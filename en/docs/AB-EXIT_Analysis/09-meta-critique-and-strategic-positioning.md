## 96. The Structural Hypocrisy of Academic Criticism — A Double Standard for the 48% Non-Voting Norm and AB-EXIT

### 96.1 — Symmetric Situation, Asymmetric Reaction

Modern political science calmly states a fact: in national elections in developed democracies, a stable 35-55% of voters do not show up at the polls. In the US, the 2020 election turnout was 66% (a 120-year record), in the 2022 midterms — about 47%, and in odd-year local elections — 15-25%. In Russia, the 2021 Duma elections saw 51.7%; in Moldova, the 2025 parliamentary elections had 48.7%; in France, the 2024 parliamentary elections saw about 67% in the first round and a decline in the second. This is the **basic given** upon which all modern electoral sociology is built. This is the **norm**.

The academic language around this norm is sterile. "Electoral apathy," "civic alienation," "erosion of civic participation," "decline in voter turnout in post-industrial democracies." Each of these terms turns mass non-participation into a **studied phenomenon**. None of the political scientists takes the podium and says: "Forty-eight percent of our fellow citizens are excluded from democracy; this is a catastrophe, we must act immediately." It is a normal background. It is a given.

But as soon as AB-EXIT appears, the very same academic circle immediately shifts its register. The same forty-eight percent of non-participation, provided the state recognizes this non-participation and pays compensation for it, turns into a **"state policy to exclude citizens from elections,"** into a **"modern poll tax,"** into **"buying silence,"** and into a **"threat to democracy."**

What exactly has changed? The behavior of citizens is the same. The electoral result is the same. The distribution of votes among candidates is the same. Absolutely nothing has changed, except for **transparency and compensation**. The state has ceased to be a blind witness to non-voting and has become a participant in a transaction with the citizen.

And this is where academic neutrality breaks down.

### 96.2 — Where the Academics are Right

Denis Kliavlin, as the author of the protocol, admits honestly: the academics are **right on one point**. AB-EXIT indeed transforms what was a sociological phenomenon into state policy. Previously, non-voting was the result of multiple personal decisions, uncoordinated, unpaid, and unaccounted for by the state. Now, non-voting becomes a **legal option** that the state officially offers to every citizen and pays for.

This is a new structural fact, and to ignore it would be dishonest.

The state in AB-EXIT does not remain neutral. It **actively offers** the citizen a deal: "Here is your choice — vote with increased weight or take compensation for non-participation." This is no longer simply an acknowledgment of apathy — it is the formation of an institutional alternative to voting.

There is no sense in denying the obvious here. AB-EXIT is a state policy to legitimize the choice between two forms of participation in the political process. Voting is one form. Compensated exit is another form. Both are approved by the state. Both are officially recognized.

### 96.3 — Where the Academics are Wrong

But between "a state policy legitimizing choice" and "vote buying" lies a structural abyss that critics either do not see or intentionally ignore.

**Vote buying** is a transaction in which one party (a party, a candidate, an oligarch) pays the other party (the voter) for a **specific direction of their choice**. "Vote for Ivanov and you will get buckwheat." "Do not vote for Petrov and you will get an envelope." This is a criminal offense in almost all legal systems in the world because it distorts the electoral will in favor of the one who paid.

**AB-EXIT** is a transaction in which the state pays the citizen for the **recognition of their own choice**. The citizen independently chooses between two options — a vote with increased weight or compensation. The state does not direct their choice in favor of any candidate. The state **recognizes the result of any choice**. If the citizen chooses to vote, the state amplifies their vote. If they choose compensation, the state pays. In both cases, the citizen receives something of value. No candidate directly wins from their choice.

This is structurally a **different class of relations**. And here the academics have nothing to say.

They might object that the compensation will influence the citizen's choice — someone will take the money, someone will stay to vote. This is true. But it is not **vote buying** in a legal sense. It is the **creation of conditions for an informed choice**. Just as a state program supporting small businesses creates conditions for choosing entrepreneurial activity — but does not "buy entrepreneurs."

### 96.4 — Structural Novelty — This Simply Did Not Exist Before

The main reason for academic panic regarding AB-EXIT is not that the protocol violates any specific democratic principles. The reason is deeper — **AB-EXIT belongs to a class of institutional relations that previously did not exist**.

In the history of democracy, there have been the following classes:

— **Free participation** — the citizen votes at the cost of their own time, receiving no compensation. This has been the norm for the last 150 years.

— **Free non-participation** — the citizen does not vote, and no one pays either them or for them. This is the norm for 35-55% today.

— **Compulsory participation** — the state obliges the citizen to vote under threat of a fine (Australia, Belgium). The state pays with a fine, the citizen pays with obedience.

— **Illegal vote buying** — illegal parties pay the citizen for a specific choice. This is a crime.

AB-EXIT creates a **new, fifth class of relations**: **legal compensated non-participation via a state mechanism**. This class has never existed in an institutional form. Therefore, academic democratic theory does not have ready-made categories for it.

And here lies the key. **The absence of a category does not equal the absence of legitimacy**. When state unemployment insurance first appeared at the beginning of the 20th century, academic economics had no category for it. It was declared "socialism," "the corruption of the workforce," "a distortion of the labor market." Fifty years passed, the category of the "welfare state" emerged, and unemployment insurance became the norm in all developed countries.

When civil rights for women first appeared, academic law had no category for "gender equality." It was declared "unnatural," a "threat to the family," a "political adventure." Fifty years passed, the category emerged, and equality became the norm.

AB-EXIT is at this historical juncture. The institutional form outpaces the academic language. **This is not a flaw of AB-EXIT. It is a flaw of the language** with which they attempt to criticize it.

### 96.5 — Where the Money Goes Now

An additional angle that academic criticism usually ignores: the state **already pays** for every vote and every instance of non-participation. It’s just that the recipients of these payments are not the citizens themselves.

State funding of political parties in most European countries is tied to election results. A party receives a few euros or dollars **for every vote cast for it** from the state budget. In Germany, this is about 1 euro per vote for a party that garners more than 0.5%. In France, it is about 1.4 euros per vote. Moldova, Poland, and the Baltic states have similar schemes. That is, the state **already recognizes the monetary value of a vote** and **already pays for it**.

The costs of holding elections — paying election commissions, renting premises, ballots, security, observers — are funded from the state budget, i.e., from the taxes of all citizens, including the 48% who do not vote. These citizens **are already paying for their non-attendance** through taxes; they just never know it.

The industry of political consultants, political technologists, political advertising, media campaigns — is estimated in the US at $26+ billion per electoral cycle, in Europe at $5-8 billion. This money does not come from voters — it comes from parties, corporations, lobbying groups, and ultimately from taxpayers through corporate expenses and state subsidies of political processes. Thus, **the cash flow around each vote already exists**.

AB-EXIT does not create expenses out of thin air. It **redirects existing flows**. A portion of the money that currently goes to parties, political technologists, and the media industry for working with the electorate is returned in AB-EXIT **directly to the citizen**, who is the owner of the vote.

When an academic critic says, "AB-EXIT is a misuse of the budget," the author of the protocol has a simple answer: **the budget is already being spent on political processes**, and it is being spent in favor of the political class, not in favor of the citizen. AB-EXIT simply changes the recipient.

### 96.6 — Why Academic Theory Cannot Accept AB-EXIT

If the academics are right that AB-EXIT is a new state policy, but wrong that it is vote buying, and if economically it does not create expenses out of nowhere but redirects existing ones — then where does the moral sharpness of the reaction come from?

The underlying reason is that **the academic theory of modern democracy rests on the myth of universal participation**. In this theory, democracy is "the power of all citizens," embodied through regular free elections with high turnout. When real turnout is 47% or 52%, academics explain this gap through concepts of "temporary apathy," "structural barriers to participation," "erosion of civic virtues." These concepts only work as long as non-participation remains **invisible and unpaid**.

As soon as the state officially recognizes non-participation, pays compensation for it, and makes the citizen's choice transparent, the myth of universal participation **is publicly shattered**. It becomes visible: half of the citizens **consciously** do not participate, and this is not temporary apathy, it is a stable position, formalized through a market transaction with the state.

This **destroys the fundamental basis of the academic language of democracy**. Therefore, the reaction of the academic circle to AB-EXIT is inevitably sharp — not out of moral considerations, but out of an instinct for self-preservation of the scientific paradigm. AB-EXIT exposes the fictitious nature of the subject studied by political science over the last 70 years.

The democracy that academics describe in textbooks does not match the democracy that exists in practice. They defend the theory. AB-EXIT shows reality.

### 96.8 — What Academic Criticism Lacks

If we structure everything stated above — academic criticism of AB-EXIT lacks a single solid argument that does not fall apart upon direct collision with facts.

There is no argument about **exclusion from democracy** — because 48% are already excluded, and academics themselves state this as a norm.

There is no argument about **budgetary expenses** — because the state already spends enormous amounts of money on political processes; AB-EXIT merely redirects the recipients.

There is no argument about **vote buying** — because AB-EXIT does not direct the choice in favor of a candidate, but recognizes the choice already made by the citizen.

There is no argument about **inequality of participation** — because AB-EXIT offers every citizen the same choice, without discrimination by income, gender, race, or education.

There is no argument about **a threat to legitimacy** — because elections with AB-EXIT remain free, secret, and competitive. Only the structure of the electorate changes — those who remain are motivated.

Academic criticism is left with only one real argument: **"this has never happened before."** And this is **true**. This really did not exist before.

But "never happened before" is not an argument. It is simply a statement of historical novelty. Universal suffrage never happened before. Freedom of the press never happened before. The right to property for former serfs never happened before. Each of these innovations caused academic panic at the moment of its appearance. And each became the norm.

AB-EXIT is simply the next institution in this chain. When academic criticism has no arguments left other than "this never happened before," it signifies not the weakness of the proposal, but the exhaustion of the language of criticism.

Then one of two things happens: either the academic language expands and begins to describe AB-EXIT in new categories (as it expanded for the welfare state, for women's rights, for basic income), or AB-EXIT is implemented through political practice, bypassing academic accompaniment, as has been the case with many reforms over the last two hundred years.

Denis Kliavlin, as the author of the protocol, does not claim that AB-EXIT is the final solution or a universal truth. He claims one thing: **AB-EXIT is a structurally new institutional class that should be tested in one state through a referendum, and not banned through academic rhetoric based on categories that do not apply to it**.

### 96.9 — Expanding the Hypocrisy: The Declaration of Greater Participation vs. The Fear of Greater Participation

The double standard described above (the norm of 48% non-voting vs. the panic over AB-EXIT) has a deeper structural parallel. The modern academic and political establishment holds **two declarations simultaneously**, which on the surface sound aligned, but in practice are incompatible.

**Declaration A — academic, public**: "Democracy is in crisis. We need more civic participation. Parties no longer work. Citizens are alienated from power. The solution lies in greater participation, in more direct democracy, in tools that bring the citizen closer to decisions." This declaration is voiced by Schulmann in her lectures, Golosov in his articles, Fukuyama in recent years, and the organizers of citizens' assemblies in Ireland and France. This is the public rhetoric of political science over the last twenty years.

**Declaration B — elite, usually unspoken**: "The citizen is too demanding. If we give them even more tools for participation, they will start demanding the impossible — better governance, transparent budgets, real accountability, a share in political revenues. It is better to keep them in the standard electoral framework — come in, vote once every four years, leave, wait for the next cycle. Direct participation creates uncertainty." This is what is said among themselves at closed forums, in memos from think tanks for corporate clients, in advisory meetings of political consultants.

The structural problem is that **these two declarations often emanate from the same people or the same institutions**. An academic writes a public lecture in the spirit of A and then advises a corporation or a party in the spirit of B. The Brookings Institution publishes reports on the need for greater participation and simultaneously works on how to keep the electorate in a manageable framework. The Council on Foreign Relations speaks of a "crisis of trust" and simultaneously supports the delegation of critical decisions to technocratic bodies, which **reduces** real participation.

AB-EXIT hits right into this contradiction. It is the realization of Declaration A **in its purest form**. Every citizen, before every election, personally, with their own hands, makes a structural choice with tangible consequences. This is the maximum of direct participation possible within a functioning state system. It is even more direct participation than Swiss referendums — because in the Swiss model the citizen votes on a specific issue, whereas in AB-EXIT they themselves determine their status in the political system for a cycle.

At the same time, AB-EXIT creates **exactly the class of citizens** that Declaration B fears. A citizen with a real economic stake in political decisions. A citizen who understands that their choice has a concrete price. A citizen who learns through the dividend that their vote is materially worth something, and begins to demand more — better governance, better transparency, better accountability.

Therefore, when an academic or elite says "AB-EXIT will create a precedent of civic demands," he or she is, in a **single sentence, admitting two things at once**:

First — admitting that **citizens are currently not demanding enough**. That is, the current balance of participation and passivity suits them. Declaration A was a slogan.

Second — admitting that they are **afraid of citizens who will be more demanding**. That is, "more participation" is good only as long as it does not become a reality. Declaration B is the true position.

**Ready-made formulation for debating an academic**:

"Let me record what you just said. You object to AB-EXIT because it will create a precedent of civic demands. The citizen, seeing that the state recognizes their choice and compensates them for it, will begin to demand greater political participation. Wait. Professor Schulmann in her lecture calls the growth of civic participation the direction of reinventing democracy. Brookings and the Council on Foreign Relations publish reports about the crisis of trust and the need for citizen engagement. What you call the risk of AB-EXIT is the **direct realization of what the academic and political establishment publicly demands**. Do you object to AB-EXIT because it works **too well** in the direction you yourself declare? Then take back the declaration. Say it honestly: you do not want more participation. You want the rhetoric of greater participation while maintaining the current level of control. That is a different position. It can be discussed. But not simultaneously with the rhetoric about reinventing democracy."

This is the second level of hypocrisy. The first level was between the status of 48% non-participation (norm) and the status of 48% compensated non-participation (threat). The second level is between the declared desire for greater participation and the fear of its real form.

Social networks and the information revolution of the last 15 years **have exposed this gap**. The citizen now sees how an academic writes one thing in a public lecture and advises a corporation differently. Sees how a politician talks about "hearing you" and votes against it. When Schulmann says in her lecture that the reinvention of democracy "has already found its answer somewhere, but we do not see it yet" — she unwittingly describes her own situation. The answer exists. But the academic circle cannot accept it because accepting this answer would require **abandoning a convenient declarative position**.

AB-EXIT is the answer that **forces the academic and elite class to choose**: either they are honest with their declaration of greater participation and support a specific mechanism, or they step out of the role of defenders of democracy and tell the truth — the current balance suits them, changes frighten them. This dilemma is presented to each representative of the class personally at the moment they encounter the specific proposal of AB-EXIT, and there is no third position into which one can hide without losing one of the two declarations.

## 97. The Arsenal of Reasonable Elites Against Populism and AB-EXIT's Place in This Landscape

### 97.1 — Whom We Call Reasonable Elites and Why Their Interests Matter

In this section, **reasonable elites** refers to a specific class of people, distinct from the populist party leadership or the corrupt apparatus. These are people with a long planning horizon who have invested in building a functioning country and fear losing it. They include: mid-sized and large-scale entrepreneurs not dependent on state contracts; university rectors and heads of research centers; chairmen of central banks and heads of independent regulators; former high-ranking ministers and diplomats; board members of large foundations and endowments; leaders of the quality press; leading constitutional and corporate law attorneys; leaders of professional associations.

Their core interests are stable and clear:

**Protection from populist outbursts.** A populist can, in one term, destroy institutions that took 30-50 years to build. Since 2010, Orbán has dismantled the Hungarian constitutional system through a stable supermajority. Brexit was passed by 52% of voters, the consequences being a decade of economic and political losses for the UK. Trump in 2024 signed a series of executive orders in his first 100 days of a second term destroying the international obligations of the US that had been forming since 1945.

**Preservation of the system's legitimacy.** Without legitimacy, any system crumbles from within in the medium term. This is the basic knowledge of any reasonable elite — you cannot govern a country that the majority does not recognize.

**Maintenance of economic stability.** They own businesses, real estate, and human capital that require a predictable environment for realization.

**Protection of the legal system.** They operate through contracts, patents, and corporate law. The destruction of the legal system is a direct loss of their core assets.

**Preservation of the possibility for long-term investments.** Their main assets are realized over 10-20-30 years. Short-term political cycles, which turn the rules of the game upside down every cycle, make long-term planning impossible.

These five interests constitute the **systemic position of reasonable elites**. They are not ideological — they are structural. A left-wing or right-wing reasonable elite is equally interested in protecting institutions from populist takeover.

### 97.2 — A Complete List of Mechanisms They Employ

Over the past forty years, reasonable elites have developed and applied approximately six main classes of mechanisms to protect against populist takeovers. Let's examine each separately.

**Institutional brakes.** Constitutional courts, separation of powers, supreme courts, the filibuster in the US Senate, supermajority requirements for constitutional changes, special impeachment procedures. All of these work as a **decelerator** — they provide time to react, hindering a rapid seizure of power.

**Delegating critical decisions to technocratic bodies.** The European Central Bank, the Federal Reserve System, the Bank of England, constitutional courts, regulatory agencies, independent electoral commissions. A massive apparatus for isolating critical decisions from populist pressure. The Singapore model is built almost entirely on this principle.

**An educated middle class and a quality press.** A long-term mechanism that worked during the period from 1945 to 2000. The expansion of university education, support for quality journalism, a factual basis for decision-making. This is an example of a successful long-term investment by post-war elites in political stability.

**Tactical electoral reforms.** Ranked Choice Voting, open primaries, mail-in voting, automatic voter registration, lowering the voting age. These are tools of the last 30 years, tested in several jurisdictions.

**Lobbying, corporate money, media machines.** This is short-term tactical defense. Big business can fund campaigns against the most dangerous populists, and media conglomerates can create pressure.

**Covert methods.** Voter ID laws, voter purges, gerrymandering. Technically applied (primarily in the US). Sometimes presented as the work of reasonable elites, but effectively an instrument of party machines.

### 97.3 — The Structural Weakness of Each Mechanism

Each of the six mechanisms has its own specific weakness, which has been exposed over the past 15 years in concrete cases.

**Institutional brakes decelerate, but do not prevent.** They work as long as a populist does not gain control for a sufficiently long term. Orbán gained a stable two-thirds of parliament three times in a row and consistently dismantled all brakes. Institutional brakes are effective against a short populist burst, ineffective against sustained populist rule.

**Delegating to technocrats cures one disease and breeds another.** Technically it works — critical decisions are made outside populist pressure. But this creates a **legitimation crisis**. People feel that important decisions are being made by "independent bodies" that no one elected. This in turn **fuels populism**. The populist's main narrative — "they weren't elected, they rule us" — works precisely because technocrats are indeed unelected. Delegation as a defense against populism paradoxically becomes the fuel for populism.

**An educated middle class and a quality press are partially broken technologically.** This mechanism worked in the era of mass media — three TV channels, a few newspapers, a shared factual baseline. In the era of social networks with algorithmic echo chambers, this mechanism is partially destroyed. The educated electorate is segregated into information bubbles, the quality press loses its audience, facts are replaced by "alternative facts." An educated person in 2025 can consume political information just as unconsciously as an uneducated one. The technological shift has undermined the main long-term tool of the reasonable elites.

### 97.4 — Four Unique Properties of AB-EXIT

AB-EXIT is unique in this landscape precisely because of its combination of four properties, which is absent in all six existing mechanisms.

**The first property — structural, not tactical.** AB-EXIT changes the **composition of the electorate**, not the rules of counting. #### Empirical Effects of Existing Mechanisms

Systematically across main tools, with concrete figures from scientific literature and empirical data.

**Electronic voting** (Estonia, since 2005). Long-term studies by Solvak & Vassil from the University of Tartu show that online voting raised overall turnout by **2-4 percentage points** after 10 years of implementation. Most online voters are people who already voted offline before, simply switching to a more convenient channel. Attracting truly new voters is about **1-2%**.

**Mail-in voting** (Oregon entirely on mail-in since 2000, expanded in the US during the pandemic). Comparing turnout before and after implementation, controlling for other factors (Berinsky 2001, Gerber et al. 2013): a turnout increase of **2-7%**. In the context of US national elections, where turnout fluctuates between 50% and 67%, this means an addition of 5-7 million votes — noticeable, but not structural.

**Automatic voter registration** (AVR). A 2019 Brookings Institution analysis of nine US states showed an increase in turnout of **2-3%** among the previously unregistered. This is about a 1-2% increase in overall turnout.

**Lowering voting age to 16** (Austria since 2007, Scotland for its own elections). Zeglovits & Aichholzer 2014 study: 16-17-year-old teenagers vote at approximately the same frequency as adults 18-21. The addition to the electorate is roughly **2-4%** of the total number of voters, but not a structural change.

**Compulsory voting** (Australia, Belgium, and about 22 other countries). This is the strongest mechanism in terms of effect — mandatory attendance plus fines yield **+25-35% to turnout**. Australia stably maintains a 90-95% turnout. But this is not a solution to the problem of apathy and populism — it merely masks it. The compulsory electorate includes a huge percentage of random, protest, and emotional votes. Populist parties (One Nation, recent fatigue with Labor) regularly win in Australia. The effect on the quality of electoral decisions is close to zero or negative (Brennan, "The Ethics of Voting", 2011).

**Ranked Choice Voting** (Maine since 2018, Alaska since 2022, about 50 US cities). The best data is Alaska 2022. The effect on populism exists: moderate Democrat Mary Peltola beat populist Sarah Palin thanks to the redistribution of second choices. But this is a local, contextual effect. The structural effect on the composition of the electorate is zero. The structural effect on reducing populist mobilization is **0-3% on average across elections**.

**Open primaries** (California Top-Two, Alaska, Louisiana). Grose & Yoshinaka 2003 and newer work McGhee et al. 2014: open primaries reduce the share of radical candidates in the final vote by about **5-8%**, but do not affect the composition of the electorate.

**Citizens' assemblies** (Ireland 2017, France 2020). An effect of a completely different order — it is a tool for making specific decisions (abortion in Ireland, climate in France), not an electoral reform. Not directly comparable to AB-EXIT. The effect on electoral processes is **0%**.

**Democracy vouchers** (Seattle since 2017). Long-term studies by the Brennan Center 2020: expansion of participation in campaign financing from 1.5% to 4.4% of residents. This is +3% to participation in a specific sense — financing, not voting. The impact on the composition of the electorate is close to zero.

**Education and quality press** (historical long-term mechanism). Putnam in "Bowling Alone" 2000 estimated the growth in turnout over the post-war period at approximately **5-7% over 50 years** as a result of the expansion of university education. But this effect is now reversed: turnout is falling, trust in the press is falling, polarization is growing. The current effect is negative.

**Lobbying, corporate money, media machines.** Empirical data on the impact of media campaigns on electoral outcomes (Bartels 2008, Gerber-Karlan-Bergan 2009): the effect of a massive media campaign on specific results is about a **1-2%** shift at huge costs. This is the reason why Hillary Clinton lost to Trump with a tenfold financial advantage — the effect of a media machine is weak with a structurally opposed audience.

## 98. AB-EXIT as a Mechanism for Releasing Accumulated Pressure — A Paradigm Shift from Obligation to Choice

### 98.1 — Modern Politics as a Machine Producing Resentment Without an Outlet

Before discussing what AB-EXIT does, one must accurately describe what **current politics does to a person**. Not ideologically, but psychologically — what an ordinary citizen feels in a modern democratic system, and what structure this feeling creates.

A citizen pays taxes. They see how they are spent (or not spent). They observe corruption, unfulfilled promises, politicians enriching themselves at their expense. They gradually accumulate **justified resentment**. It is not fabricated by populists, not manipulative — it is a real, factually confirmed resentment toward a system that treats them unfairly.

This resentment is unlike anger directed at a specific person. It is **diffuse pressure**, directed against the system as a whole. The citizen cannot name a specific culprit — "all of them," "this whole system," "politicians," "elites" are to blame. This pressure lacks a specific addressee, making it difficult to resolve directly.

And this resentment **finds no legitimate channel** for its articulation. The modern democratic system has no institutional tool through which a citizen could publicly, within respected procedures, formalize their stance: "I disagree with how this system treats me, and I refuse to participate in its rituals." Any such attempt is interpreted by the system as **dysfunction**: apathy, irresponsibility, the erosion of civic culture. The system needs the citizen to **remain within its procedures**, even if they are dissatisfied.

This creates a **psychological trap**. The citizen can neither participate normally (because they don't believe in the outcome), nor not participate normally (because society stigmatizes non-participation). They are stuck between two bad options, and every election cycle **deepens this state of being stuck**.

### 98.2 — The Citizen's Three Options — All Three Keep the Steam Inside the System

The modern political structure leaves the dissatisfied citizen with three behavioral options. Let me analyze each and show why all three **fail to resolve the accumulated resentment**, but only transfer it into new forms.

**Option one — continue voting for "your" candidate.** The citizen votes for the party or candidate most appealing to them. This candidate wins or loses, but in either case, the **base structure of the system does not change**. The same institutions, the same distributions of power and money, the same rhythms of corruption and unfulfilled promises. The citizen lets off a bit of steam through the act of voting (providing brief relief — "I did what I could"), but then sees the result and **the resentment returns**, often amplified by disappointment.

This cycle repeats every four years. Every time, the citizen hopes for the best, votes, receives disappointment, and waits for the next cycle. Resentment accumulates **layer by layer**, because the system provides no tool for its radical resolution.

**Option two — stop voting silently.** The citizen decides they no longer want to partake in a spectacle where their participation changes nothing. They stay home. But they receive a **double negative effect**.

The first is social stigma. Peers, media, academics, relatives say: "you're apathetic," "you're irresponsible," "people like you destroy democracy," "if you don't vote, you have no right to complain." This pressure is genuine from some, manipulative from others, but in all cases, it creates a **constant background discomfort**. The citizen feels they must justify not doing something they dislike.

The second is a feeling of their own powerlessness. The citizen knows their silent non-participation is unheard by anyone. They gave up the only channel through which they could theoretically be heard — voting. Now they are **simply silent**, and their silence communicates nothing to the system other than the statistic "turnout dropped."

This is the worst option in terms of psychological resolution. The resentment **is multiplied by shame** for non-participation, and both feelings rot inside the citizen without any outlet.

**Option three — vote for a populist.** The citizen votes for a candidate promising to "tear it all down," "kick out the elites," "return the country to the people." It is important to understand: the citizen votes for a populist **not because they believe the promises**. They often fully realize the populist won't be able to fulfill anything, that many of their promises are fantastical or harmful. They vote for the populist because it is the **only way to officially, within the democratic procedure, let off steam**. It is an act of public protest framed as a vote.

The effect is short-lived. The populist gains power or does not. If they do — they either cannot accomplish anything (and the resentment returns with double force a cycle later), or they begin to genuinely destroy institutions (and the country suffers real losses, which in the long run hit that very same citizen). If they don't gain power — the citizen feels the elites have rigged the system again, and the resentment deepens.

All three options **keep the steam inside the system**. It accumulates from cycle to cycle, from generation to generation. Every subsequent generation inherits more unresolved pressure than the last.

### 98.3 — The Geological Pressure of Accumulated Resentments as an Explanation for the Rise of Populism

If we look at the political history of the Western world from 2010 to 2025 through this prism, the picture becomes clear.

Brexit 2016, Trump 2016 and 2024, Orbán from 2010 onwards, the Freedom Party in Austria, AfD in Germany, Le Pen in France, Brothers of Italy 2022, Milei in Argentina, the rise of Reform UK in 2025 — these are not random political events. Citizens did not vote for the specific programs of populists (which were often contradictory or unachievable). They voted for the **chance to let off steam through the only available channel**. A Brexit voter, choosing to leave the EU, more often than not had no detailed plan for the UK's trade relations post-exit. They were voting for an **act of defiance** against a global system in which they felt disenfranchised.

The Trump voter in 2016 and 2024 wasn't voting for specific tariffs or immigration policies. ### 98.9 — Three Reasons Why a Centrist Speech with AB-EXIT is Stronger Than a Populist One

**The first — it is actionable.** A populist promises the destruction of elites, a return to a utopian past, an instant solution to complex structural problems. These promises are **systemically unachievable**. When a populist comes to power, they cannot fulfill them, which begins a new cycle of resentment among their voters. A centrist with AB-EXIT promises concrete things — compensation, amplified vote weight, a cyclical choice. These things are **technically actionable** in one state via ballot initiative. When a promise is fulfilled, trust in the centrist grows, not falls. This is a sustainable model of political capital.

**The second — it is positive.** The populist offers resolution through anger and destruction. They cultivate an image of an enemy — elites, immigrants, liberals, conservatives, depending on the brand of populism. To remain a supporter of the populist, the citizen must **sustain a state of anger**. This is emotionally exhausting. After a few years, even the most devoted supporters of a populist begin to burn out.

A centrist with AB-EXIT offers resolution through **recognition and material return**. A citizen does not have to hate anything to receive the compensation. They simply state their position honestly — and the state respects it. This is a **psychologically more sustainable** position that does not require maintaining negative emotions. A citizen can remain a supporter of this system for a long time because it does not require emotional exhaustion from them.

**The third — it is respectful.** The populist says "you are victims, your enemies are the elites." This is patronizing — addressing the citizen as an aggrieved child in need of a protector. The citizen accepts this role temporarily because no one else offers them a positive self-image. But in the long term, a permanent victim role is **destructive** to self-respect.

A centrist with AB-EXIT says "you are adults, choose for yourselves, we respect both options." This is an **appeal to dignity**, not to resentment. The citizen gains a positive self-image — they are not a victim, they are a subject of choice, recognized by the state. And in the long run, **dignity is stronger than resentment**. A citizen who feels their dignity does not descend into radicalization.

This third reason is the deepest. Populism feeds on the humiliation of citizens by elites, accumulating it into collective anger. AB-EXIT, through a state act of recognition, **breaks the cycle of humiliation**, depriving populism of its basic fuel. Not through fighting populists, but through **healing the condition upon which they parasitize**.

### 98.10 — Reevaluating What AB-EXIT Is

This section redefines the project.

Until this section, we described AB-EXIT as a **structural mechanism design for the electoral system**. This is academically accurate, but emotionally dead. This positioning works for reasonable elites and the tech audience, accustomed to thinking quantitatively. But it **does not explain why AB-EXIT can become a mass phenomenon**.

After this section, we say: **AB-EXIT is the first mechanism in history for state recognition of citizen resentment, offering a concrete act of fairness in exchange for their honest acknowledgment of their stance**.

This is a completely different project. And this different project has an enormous mobilization potential that the first formulation lacks.

Electoral effects — the decline of populism, the expansion of the planning horizon, the filtering of apathetic voters, the protection of institutions against capture — are **consequences**. The **root** is in the emotional resolution of accumulated pressure through a symbolic act of state respect toward the citizen.

It is important to secure this reformulation on several levels.

**On the level of communication.** The base messages of the project must be reformulated. Not "vote or take the money" — but "the state recognizes your choice for the first time." Not "AB-EXIT is an electoral system reform" — but "AB-EXIT is the first political tool that treats you with respect." Not "we fight populism" — but "we resolve the accumulated pressure upon which populism parasitizes."

**On the level of the headline message.** The current formulation "Your vote. Your money. Your exit." works in a paradigm of obligation — "exit" implies "exiting an obligation." This is correct, but insufficient. Variants in the new paradigm:

— "AB-EXIT. Your choice. Your value. Your dignity." — about the dignity of choice.
— "AB-EXIT. Vote with weight. Or live free. Your call." — about freedom.
— "AB-EXIT. Politics that respects you." — short, emotional.

The exact final formulation should be tested via A/B experiments with a real audience. But the direction is clear — away from "exit" toward **"respect"** or **"dignity"**.

**On the level of the target audience.** Prior to this section, the primary addressees were reasonable elites and tech capital — people for whom quantitative arguments work. After this section, the **mass audience** is added — millions of people for whom the emotional argument about state recognition works. These are **different campaigns** with different language, but both are needed.

For reasonable elites — the quantitative language of section 97: 20-40% effect versus 2-8% of existing mechanisms. For the mass audience — the emotional language of section 98: the state respects you for the first time, let off steam legally, reclaim your dignity.

These are two campaigns working synergistically. Reasonable elites provide **funding and legitimation**. The mass audience provides **votes in a referendum**. Without one, there is not the other. And both campaigns must be run in parallel from the very beginning.

**On the level of the core AB-EXIT slogan.** Perhaps the basic short formulation of the project should be rewritten to carry the emotional charge of this paradigm. The current "A — I vote. B — I take the money. One exit" is a technical description of the mechanism. The new variant could be:

"AB-EXIT. The state reckons with you for the first time. Vote with increased weight, or take compensation for honesty. You choose. No one owes you anything anymore — and you owe no one."

Or even shorter: "AB-EXIT. Politics where you're finally treated like an adult."

These formulations might seem overly emotional for intellectual circles — but they work for a mass audience. And it is precisely the mass audience that votes in the referendum introducing AB-EXIT into a given state. Without the emotional mobilization of millions, a referendum does not pass, and all the technical mechanism design remains in the offices of academics and venture capitalists.

### Final Thesis

AB-EXIT is not an electoral reform in the traditional sense. It is the **first institutional gesture of the state toward the citizen framed as respect, rather than a demand**.

If AB-EXIT is understood this way, it becomes clear why it is capable of resolving the crisis of modern democracy. Not through yet another vote-counting mechanism or filtering of the electorate. But through **altering the fundamental emotional structure of relations** between the citizen and the political system.

A citizen who, for the first time in their life, is **respected** by the state, relates to that state differently. They do not vote for demagogues, because they harbor no accumulated resentment against the system. They do not turn to radicalism, because their dignity is already recognized. They are capable of compromise, because they don't feel trapped in a victim's role. They build long-term plans, because they believe the system reckons with them.

This is exactly what a healthy democracy is. And AB-EXIT is the way to get it. Not by fighting symptoms, but by **healing the fundamental relationship** between the state and the citizen.
