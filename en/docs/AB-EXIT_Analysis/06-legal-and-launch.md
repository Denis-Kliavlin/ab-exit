## 68. Legal Framework for U.S. Launch: Findings from the Third Round of Adversarial Review

### 68.1. Purpose of this Section

As part of the stress test of the pilot plan through Gemini's adversarial review (Round 3), specific legal facts were obtained that narrow and refine the launch route. This section records confirmed objective data (statutes, precedents, figures) and, separately, the methodological limitations of LLM assessment for unprecedented strategic questions.

### 68.2. State Map: Where Citizen Initiatives Bypass Blocking

In 26 U.S. states, citizen ballot initiatives are permitted. Among them, those suitable for an AB-EXIT initiative are those where substantive pre-election review is prohibited:

**Permissive (Signature collection and ballot access possible, challenges only post-election):**
- **Oregon** — *Foster v. Clark* (1990): courts cannot remove an initiative from the ballot on constitutional grounds; review is only procedural.
- **California** — *AFL v. Eu* (1984): pre-election review is prohibited, except in cases where it is "invalid on its face" per procedure.
- **Colorado** — The Title Board only checks the "single-subject" rule (State Constitution, Art. V § 1).
- **Washington, Arizona** — Similar regimes.

**Restrictive (Blocking before signature collection, unsuitable for AB-EXIT):**
- **Massachusetts** — The State Attorney General is required to block an initiative if it violates fundamental rights (Mass. Const. Amend. Art. 48).
- **Florida** — The State Supreme Court reviews the text of the initiative before the vote.
- **Arkansas** — Strict substantive review.

### 68.3. Precedents for Changing Election Law via Citizen Initiative

Citizen initiatives have successfully changed structural electoral laws:

- **Florida Amendment 4 (2018)** — Restored voting rights to 1.4 million convicted felons, radically changing the status of "electoral offender" in the state.
- **Colorado Amendment 27 (2002)** — Campaign contribution limits.
- **South Dakota IM 22 (2016)** — Radical revision of bribery/lobbying laws (subsequently repealed by the legislature).
- **North Dakota Measure 1 (2018)** — Transparency reforms.

**Important:** As of 2026, there are no precedents for the direct *legalization* of what was previously considered election bribery. However, the structural change of the criminal framework of elections through initiative is a matter of routine, not an anomaly.

### 68.4. The Death of Form B: Legislative Mandamus

The alternative form "citizen initiative requires the legislature to hold hearings / create a commission / issue an advisory question" is legally null in most states. This is a violation of the separation of powers (legislative mandamus) — a statute cannot command the legislative branch what to do. Courts (including in Oregon and California) immediately overturn such provisions.

Exception: Illinois allows non-binding advisory questions via citizen petitions. In other states, an advisory question can only be issued by the legislature itself.

**Conclusion:** Form A (direct change of the vote-bribery statute via citizen initiative) is the only realistic path. Form B will not survive in court.

### 68.5. Preemption: 10307(c) and Real Sphere of Action

If a state initiative is passed, federal 52 U.S.C. 10307(c) preempts state law in any election where a federal candidate (President, Senate, House of Representatives) is on the ballot. The basis for this is the Supremacy Clause (Constitution, Art. VI) and the Elections Clause (Art. I, § 4).

Consequence: even after successful adoption in Oregon, AB-EXIT legally works **only** in purely off-year state and local elections (odd years) where there is no federal position. In mixed elections, the mechanism is blocked by federal law.

This narrows the sphere of action but does not cancel it. Local and state self-government remains a legal testing ground.

### 68.6. Precedent: U.S. Term Limits v. Thornton (1995)

The citizens of Arkansas, via Amendment 73, introduced term limits for their Senators and Congressmen in Washington. The U.S. Supreme Court overturned this: a state cannot change federal electoral rules.

**What happened to the initiators:** nothing criminal. They lost in court but gained significant political capital and national attention. Drafting an unconstitutional law is not a crime.

This is an important precedent for AB-EXIT: even if a state initiative passes and is overturned by a federal court, the initiators are not subject to criminal prosecution — they gain a springboard of attention, exactly according to the logic of Section 62 (Overturning as a springboard).

### 68.7. Oregon: Operational Specifics

- **Signature Threshold**: 117,173 (6% of ~1.93 million votes in the 2022 gubernatorial election).
- **Geographic Quota**: None. Unlike Colorado or Nevada, Oregon has no strict distribution by county — signatures can all be collected in Portland.
- **Valid Signature Rate**: Standard 70-75%. To guarantee 117k valid signatures, a minimum of 160,000 raw signatures must be collected.
- **Submission Deadline**: Early July 2026 (for the November 2026 ballot).

This makes Oregon operationally the most convenient of the suitable states. Concentrating collection in one city sharply reduces costs and logistics.

### 68.8. Precedents of Anchor Funding (The Model Works)

The hypothesis that "one donor with a historical motive covers 70-80% of the budget for a state initiative" is not theoretical; it has worked many times:

- **Tim Draper** — $5+ million of personal funds for the "Six Californias" initiative (dividing the state).
- **Sean Parker** — ~$8 million as the anchor donor for Prop 64 (marijuana legalization in California).
- **Kent Thiry (ex-CEO of DaVita)** — Millions of dollars single-handedly for initiatives on open primaries and anti-gerrymandering in Colorado.

The profile has been found repeatedly: an entrepreneur/billionaire with a post-career drive for political experiment, choosing a non-partisan measure for a historical legacy. Finding one such anchor for AB-EXIT is a realistic task, not a utopia.

### 68.9. Real Operational Risks (Not Present in Initial Plan)

The third round identified three tactical threats, important for budgeting and preparation:

**Battle for Ballot Title.** The official name and short description (Summary) on the Oregon ballot are written by the State Attorney General. A hostile formulation ("Legalizes paying citizens to refrain from voting") can kill the initiative because a significant portion of voters only read the title. This requires a budget reserve for a legal dispute with the AG in the State Supreme Court BEFORE the main signature collection phase.

Counter-mechanic (Sections 62-63): a hostile formulation itself works as media fuel — "The AG is protecting the system from your right to choose" — but only if the project has the media machine to pivot the narrative. Without it, the hostile title hits home.

**Poison Pill from the Legislature.** The state legislature, seeing the growth in signature collection, could urgently put its own counter-measure (Referred Constitutional Amendment) on the same ballot, prohibiting any payments for refraining from voting. This creates a chaos of two mutually exclusive measures.

Counter-mechanic: a poison pill publicly makes the legislature the opponent of a popular initiative. This is an exposing move, not a neutral one — but it requires readiness from the media machine to convert it into the narrative "they are against you."

**Blockers against Signature Collectors.** Unions and the party establishment can hire people to physically accompany signature collectors with megaphones ("They want to steal your elections with billionaires' money!"). This reduces collection speed by 3-4 times and raises the cost per signature from ~$10 to ~$40. Without a budget reserve, collection stops.

All three risks are mitigated by sufficient anchor funding and an advance-prepared media machine, but they cannot be ignored.

### 68.10. Methodological Observation: Limits of LLM Adversarial Assessment

In three rounds of assessment, a steady pattern of LLM evaluator limitation on strategic questions for unprecedented situations was identified:

The LLM evaluator is STRONG on legal analysis (statutes, precedents, figures — these are objective facts with verifiable sources). In Zone A of the third round, real new data was obtained that was not in the document and which narrows/refines the route.

The LLM evaluator is WEAK on strategic forecasts of unprecedented situations. Even with explicit instructions "mark speculative, do not give categorical numbers," the LLM issues confident assessments, projecting the past (crypto-mayors, off-year demographics, typical ballot initiatives) onto a class of situation that has no analogs. This is a structural, not a prompt-based, limitation.

**Practical Conclusion:** The LLM evaluator is useful for checking legal feasibility and should be used. For checking strategic chances, it gives a false sense of accuracy — it is better to leave strategy to people with real expertise in political campaigns than to believe an LLM figure derived from unsuitable precedents.

This is not a reproach of the tool — it is a clarification of its boundaries of applicability. Apply it where it is strong; do not trust it where it is structurally weak.

### 68.11. Summary: Launch Route After the Third Round

The clean legal route (on solid ground):
1. Form A — citizen-initiated statute narrowing Oregon's vote-bribery statute through an explicit carve-out.
2. Oregon — optimal state (no content review, no geographic quota, concrete threshold of 117k signatures).
3. Signature submission deadline — early July 2026 for the November 2026 ballot.
4. Budget — hybrid: $3-5M anchor donor + mass base; the model is empirically confirmed (Draper, Parker, Thiry).
5. Sphere of action after adoption — only off-year state/local elections (odd years); federal elections are blocked by 10307(c) until federal law is changed.
6. Risk reserve — for the battle for ballot title and blockers (4x increase in signature cost).

This is not an "easy path" or a "guarantee." It is a concrete route with known obstacles and empirically confirmed precedents at every step.

- An underdog with a standard program collects 25-30% of those who showed up = 5,000-6,000 votes.

**With AB-EXIT as a platform, same city:**

- Total turnout: 45,000 instead of 20,000.
- Incumbent: shrinks to ~10,000.
- Underdog: 5,000 (old base) + 25,000 (newly mobilized) = 30,000.
- Result: ~66% vs. ~33%. A landslide victory.

The figures are approximate, but the order of magnitude is clear: AB-EXIT turns an inevitably losing race into a race with an inevitable victory, expanding the electoral base by 2-3 times.

## 78. Technical Architecture of AB-EXIT: Blockchain as Formula Protection, AI as Accelerator, Scripts as Calculator

### 78.1. Purpose of the Section

All previous 77 sections describe AB-EXIT conceptually and strategically: formula, motivation, launch route, legal framework, carrier and donor portraits, and response methodology for critics. But between the "conceptual formula" and a "working payment system," one last hole remains — technical implementation. This section closes it.

This section also serves as a precise answer to Yampolskiy-type objections (see reconstruction in Section 77 and beyond): it demonstrates the correct separation of responsibility between the human, dumb code, blockchain, and AI, in which no component receives power it is not supposed to have.

### 78.2. Fundamental Separation of Responsibility

AB-EXIT is not "managed by AI." AB-EXIT is not "entrusted to an algorithm." AB-EXIT is built on a clear separation of functions between four types of components, each with its own narrow role:

**The Human makes decisions.** Citizens decide whether to vote or take the dividend. Citizens decide whom to elect. Citizens approve the initial implementation via referendum and any changes to parameters via repeat referendum. No machine has a vote, and no machine interprets human choice. These are political decisions, and they remain with people.

**A "Dumb" Script performs calculations.** The AB-EXIT formula (D = M × 1.5 × 1%) is primary school arithmetic. AI is not needed to execute it. A deterministic script of 15 lines of code is needed: "take number M, multiply by 1.5, multiply by 0.01, return the result." This script has no "understanding," no "learning," no "optimization" — it does what is written and does nothing else. This is ideal for a distribution function.

**Blockchain records and protects.** The data source (median W-2 from SSA), the formula in the smart contract, and the history of all calculations and payments are all recorded in a publicly verifiable blockchain with a cryptographic signature from an authoritative source. One cannot "agree to use a different figure," one cannot "quietly rewrite the formula," and one cannot "forget to credit someone." Blockchain makes impossible the very "poison pill" from Section 73.

**AI accelerates and verifies.** It does not make decisions or interpret policy. Its role is technical: aggregating SSA data in real time, comparing declared municipal expenditures with actual transactions in the blockchain, identifying anomalies for citizen auditors (Section 18), and translating 147-page PDFs into readable dashboards. AI is an instrument of speed and accuracy, not a decision-making center.

### 78.3. Why a "Dumb" Script Is Safer than AI for Calculations

Paradoxically, Yampolskiy's own position on system safety works for AB-EXIT, not against it: the simpler the system, the safer it is. A dumb deterministic script is safer than an AI auditor by the same logic that a mechanical lock is safer than an electronic one with firmware.

— Fewer attack vectors. A 15-line script has no "unforeseen behavior"; its behavior is fully described by the code. AI has weights of billions of parameters; predicting behavior in edge cases is impossible.

— No "emergent effects." A script does not "learn," "optimize," or "adapt." A year later, it does exactly the same thing it did on launch day.

— Code verifiability. 15 lines can be checked by any programming school graduate. AI behavior is not fully verifiable — it is an active research problem in AI safety.

— No autonomy. A script does not make decisions "on its own behalf." It takes an input, applies a formula, and returns an output. It is a calculator, not an agent.

Where AI would be redundant and dangerous: in the calculation of the dividend itself. This is a task for a calculator, and applying AI there is creating risk without benefit. AB-EXIT architecture correctly leaves the calculation to a dumb script.

### 78.4. What Blockchain Adds to "Poison Pill" Protection

Section 73 described four ways to replace code with a single sentence. Blockchain closes each of them at the technical level, not just the statutory one:

**Protection against replacing the source (Sections 73.2-73.3).** If the median W-2 is published in a public blockchain with an SSA cryptographic signature, the figure becomes the sole truth for all municipalities. No mayor can locally use another number — everyone will see the discrepancy between the figure published in the blockchain and his calculation. Any resident can verify which figure was used in 3 clicks.

**Protection against diluting the formula (Section 73.4).** The formula is fixed in a smart contract at the first launch after a referendum. Not "may be changed by the legislature," not "requires commission approval." Change requires deploying a new version of the contract, which requires a new referendum. Bureaucracy loses the lever of quiet replacement.

**Protection against killing automatism (Section 73.5).** Payments are automatic upon registration of a voter as having taken the "dividend" option. No Equity Board can "not approve" a specific person. No union can "block the list." You made a choice — you received a transfer. The exit barrier is zero, as required by the architecture.

**Protection against data manipulation.** Cryptographic signatures make unnoticed replacement of figures impossible at any stage of transmission. If the SSA has signed "median for 2027 — $47,800," this figure cannot be changed at any point in the chain.

Blockchain does not "improve" AB-EXIT — it makes its statutory protection technically feasible. Without blockchain, the protection from Section 73 remains on paper: "the formula must not change" in the law, but in reality, it is changed through bylaws. With blockchain, protection becomes mathematical: "the formula cannot change without a public procedure with a new consensus," and this is no longer a promise, but a property of the system.

### 78.5. Where AI Actually Helps (The Role of Accelerator)

AI in AB-EXIT serves, it does not manage. Specific functions where it improves the system without seizing control:

**Real-time calculation.** Without AI, the median W-2 is published once a year with a 12-18 month lag. With AI aggregation of SSA data, the median is updated in real time as data arrives. Citizens see their potential dividend NOT in two years, but now. Transparency increases, and the feedback cycle shortens.

**Real-time audit.** AI agents constantly compare the city's declared expenditures with actual operations in the blockchain, instantly identifying anomalies. The 14,000 citizen auditors from Section 18 receive not an "annual report after the fact," but a "notification 30 seconds after a suspicious transaction." This is a tool for human auditors, not a replacement for them — the final decision on what to do with an anomaly remains with citizens and the court.

**Accuracy instead of human error.** Bureaucrats "forget" to credit, "lose" documents, "misinterpret" rules. A machine performs arithmetic without morality and without interest in the outcome. This is ideal for a distribution function where random errors can systematically hit specific groups.

**Accessibility for understanding.** AI tools can translate a 147-page PDF budget into a readable interactive dashboard where a citizen sees where their taxes are going without having to be an accountant. This removes the information barrier between the citizen and the audit.

In all four functions, AI is a tool for people, not their replacement. Decisions remain with citizens; AI simply makes information accessible and operations fast.

### 78.6. One Real Technical Risk and Comparison with the Status Quo

An honest caveat. One real technical risk still exists: vulnerabilities in the smart contract code at the development stage. The history of The DAO in 2016, Poly Network, and numerous DeFi exploits show that smart contracts can be broken, and the consequences can be catastrophic and irreversible (stolen funds cannot be returned).

**Comparison with the status quo.** Current municipal finances were also developed by humans and also contain vulnerabilities — but they are HIDE intentionally. A 147-page PDF budget, in which overpayments to contractors are encrypted between the lines, is a vulnerability by design, not by chance. Electronic voting systems, city banking systems, and city hall accounting databases — all this is already subject to attacks and already contains opaque holes.

Difference: the vulnerabilities of the current system are intentionally opaque and protected from investigation. Smart contract vulnerabilities are publicly verifiable and undergo independent audit before deployment. There is an established smart contract audit industry (OpenZeppelin, ConsenSys Diligence, Trail of Bits) with known best practices. This does not mean "no risks," but it means "risks are manageable, measurable, and publicly verifiable," which is qualitatively better than opaque mayoral accounting.

And one way to reduce even this risk: formal verification. Since the AB-EXIT smart contract handles simple arithmetic, its behavior is fully verifiable mathematically (proof that for any input M, the result equals M × 0.015). ### 80.11. The Dividend as a Common Object of Interest for All Ethnic Groups

### 80.12. The Dividend as a Measurable Metric of Management Competence in Ethnic Regions

Currently, inter-ethnic comparisons work through ideological narratives: "we work better," "we have a more developed culture," "we are more disciplined." These statements are irrefutable because there is nothing to measure them with. Each community lives in an information bubble where its achievements are exaggerated and its problems are minimized.

AB-EXIT gives a hard public metric: the size of the dividend in your region. The formula is simple, the source is external (W-2 from SSA or similar), and it cannot be manipulated. And this figure directly reflects the economic well-being of the region.

What happens in public discussion. Previously, a politician could say "we have the best economy in the Caucasus, we work perfectly" — that was a declaration. This turns inter-ethnic rivalry from a question of pride into a question of productivity. Instead of the dispute "whose culture is better," the dispute "whose management decisions are more effective" appears. And this discussion will be won not by the one who shouts loudest, but by the one who really manages their region better.

Additional mechanism: intra-state competition for competent residents. If the dividend in one ethnic region is $620 and in another $400, then an entrepreneur and a qualified specialist have a direct economic incentive to move to the first region. This creates transparent pressure on the politicians of each region from below: either you raise the median wage of your region (creating jobs, attracting investments, improving education), or your best residents leave.

This scales within the country the same mechanism that now works between countries (Singapore and Dubai golden visas, talent attraction programs — Section 80.2). And this mechanism punishes poorly managed regions and rewards well-managed ones regardless of their ethnic composition.

For any multi-ethnic country, this is the first systemic mechanism for converting identity politics into productive politics without suppressing identities themselves. Differences are preserved — the competition switches to management competence.

### 80.13. Metrics Become Living Instead of Abstract: Transition from Passive Knowledge to Active Expertise

Possible objection: "economic metrics are already public, but citizens do not look at them because they do not understand." This objection is built on the error of mixing "information does not exist" and "information exists but is not used." Currently, it is the second.

Metrics of management quality exist in abundance. Anyone can go to a statistics site and find out the median wage, regional GDP, and unemployment. These data are open; they are published by Rosstat, BLS, and analytical centers. But almost no one looks at them — and there are three structural reasons for this.

First — an ordinary person has no personal stake in the figure. It does not affect their wallet directly. They know abstractly that "things are going better or worse," but that is statistics, something for analysts and journalists.

Second — they have no moment when they MUST look at it. Life goes on, there is work, they scrolled through the news, and then they live on. The figure is available, but there is no trigger to open it.

Third — even if they open it, they don't know what to do with it. The figure of 35,000 rubles for a median wage — is it better or worse? Where is the trend? What does it mean for their life?

AB-EXIT solves all three problems simultaneously. A personal stake arises instantly: this figure is DIRECTLY your dividend. If the median is 35,000, your dividend is 525 rubles. If 60,000, your dividend is 900. This is not statistics; it is your money in your pocket.

The moment arises automatically: before each election, you must decide whether to vote or take the dividend. And this decision requires knowing how much you will receive and where the trend is going. This is an active choice every 4 years, and without knowing the figures, it cannot be made.

Understanding arises through comparison: if in your region the dividend is 525 and in the neighbor's it is 900, you immediately understand that something is wrong. No need to explain that 525 is bad abstractly. It is enough to see that the neighbor has 900.

Specific prediction: the emergence of a mass habit of analyzing public economics, which currently does not exist anywhere except among a small elite of professionals. Citizens discuss over lunch not "politicians' faces," but "the dynamics of the median wage against last year." Not "party promises," but "the real trajectory of the dividend against the neighboring region." They sit for hours before elections, counting, comparing, arguing, and estimating.

This removes one of the last serious arguments against any reform of management quality — "the citizen is incapable of understanding complex metrics." This argument applies to the current system, where the citizen has no personal stake. That same citizen perfectly understands prices in the supermarket, the cost of utility services, loan rates, and gasoline prices — they have high competence in everything that concerns their wallet directly. They are not incapable; they are not interested.

AB-EXIT gives them the same motivation for analyzing politics as they have for analyzing prices in a store. They already have the abilities — they apply them to the family budget every day. It's just that previously they did not apply them to politics because politics had no price.

## 82. AB-EXIT vs 2500-Year Tradition of Reform of Participation Quality: A Paradigmatic Shift

### 82.1. Purpose of the Section

Section 75.2 records the inversion of AB-EXIT relative to the 2500-year tradition of CENSUSES (whom to admit). This section records another inversion — relative to the 2500-year tradition of REFORM OF PARTICIPATION QUALITY (how to force those admitted to participate meaningfully). The section is needed for a specific class of conversations — with theorists of democracy, academics, the civic tech movement, and philosophers of politics. They have their own long work on the reform of participation quality, and AB-EXIT needs to be correctly positioned in relation to this work — as a PARADIGMATIC SHIFT, not as a competitor.

### 82.2. Seven Admissions of Failure

Every significant attempt at the reform of participation quality in history has ended in some form of failure. Chronology:

— **399 BC.** Socrates is executed by the democratic vote of Athens. Plato writes *The Republic* with philosopher-kings. The first recorded admission of the failure of democracy through voter education.

— **415-413 BC.** Athenian democracy with a relatively educated electorate votes for the catastrophic Sicilian expedition against the advice of Nicias. Empirical proof that deliberation does not save from collective errors.

— **133-44 BC.** The Roman Republic passes through decades of attempted reforms by the Gracchi, Cicero, and Caesar. It degrades into the principate. Third admission.

— **1789-1799.** The French Revolution begins with deliberative circles and Enlightenment philosophy. It ends with the Terror and Napoleon. Fourth admission.

— **1919-1933.** The Weimar Republic had one of the most developed political cultures in Europe, with newspapers, discussion clubs, and an educated electorate. It brought Hitler to power through normal elections. Fifth admission.

— **1962-1992.** Habermas develops "communicative action" and "deliberative democracy." Thirty years of work. Polarization and populism only grew. Sixth admission.

— **2014-2026.** Polis, vTaiwan, quadratic voting, Plurality. Works in Taiwan (a country with a unique culture, small population, and constant existential threat). Moving Polis to Bowling Green, Kentucky — 2,000 people as a pilot. On the level of a country the scale of the USA (340M), Russia (144M), or India (1.4B) — it structurally does not work. Seventh admission.

These are not "accidental failures of individual attempts." This is a steady pattern, repeating through all political cultures and eras. Such a pattern must have a structural reason.

### 82.3. The Common Erroneous Premise

All seven attempts stem from one Aristotelian premise: "man is by nature a political animal; under the right conditions, he will participate consciously."

This premise is structurally incorrect for most people in most times. Most people are by nature NOT political animals. They are animals of survival, care for loved ones, work, pleasure, culture, and love. Politics for them is an instrumental activity to which they turn ONLY when they see a direct connection with their interests. And this connection is extremely rarely obvious in a system where one vote among millions mathematically means nothing.

Plurality tries to force people to be what they are not: professional citizens. This requires from them time, cognitive resources, and emotional energy — which they either do not have, or which they spend on more urgent things. Polis can work perfectly with 2,000 motivated people in one pilot city. Polis does not scale to millions of indifferent people because the indifferent will not log into Polis in the first place.

This is the reason for the 2500-year failure: attempts to force non-professional citizens to be professional invariably fail because they go against human nature.

### 82.4. AB-EXIT as the Inversion of the Premise

AB-EXIT stems from the opposite assumption: most people normally and healthily are not interested in politics. This is not a bug; it is a feature. No need to force them. One should give them an economically rational way to exit and leave politics to those who really need and are interested in it — pragmatists and ideologues.

This is the inversion of the 2500-year paradigm. Not "how to force everyone to be smart citizens," but "how to allow those who don't want to be, not to be, with compensation for exit."

This is a paradigmatic shift, not another reform. And that is why objections developed within the old paradigm (Plurality, deliberative democracy, civic engagement) do not act on AB-EXIT — they work in a frame that AB-EXIT cancels.

### 82.5. Where Plurality Still Makes Sense — as a Superstructure over AB-EXIT

This is a critical clarification without which the dialogue with the Plurality environment turns into confrontation. AB-EXIT does not deny Polis, quadratic voting, or deliberative tools. These tools are useful — but on a LEVEL ABOVE AB-EXIT.

After AB-EXIT removes the mass of indifferent people, pragmatists (~25-30% of total population) and ideologues (~10-15%) remain. Between them, there are real nuances that require articulation. The Synthesizer Candidate (Section 72.5) should connect pragmatists and ideologues — and Polis can help in this connection. Quadratic voting can help reveal the intensity of preferences among those who really care.

Plurality is the second floor of a building built on the AB-EXIT foundation. Without a foundation, the second floor hangs in the air and collapses (which we have observed for 2500 years). With a foundation, it becomes a working construction.

Ready formulation for conversation with theorists: "Plurality deals with a symptom — low quality of participation. ## 83. From Push-Logic to Pull-Logic: AB-EXIT Turns Election Day into an Unmissable Event

### 83.1. Purpose of the Section and the Central Inversion

Before AB-EXIT, electoral infrastructure works on push-logic: "come vote." Parties, the state, the media, and activists spend billions on promoting turnout. Despite this, turnout is low, and people forget about elections, especially off-year ones. After AB-EXIT, the logic is inverted: "come get yours." Election Day ceases to be a reminder of a duty and becomes a mnemonic anchor, like payday.

This is a qualitatively separate idea, traces of which are in Sections 67 (anger industry), 75.6 (market census as an auction for the future), and 80.13 (metrics become alive). This section fixes it.

### 83.2. Why People Forget About Elections Now

Election Day is a day when a voter GIVES something (time, effort, emotional energy) and receives nothing directly. The vote of one person among millions mathematically does not affect the outcome. There is no reward. There is no punishment. This is work without pay for the majority that does not have ideological motivation.

Despite multi-billion Get Out The Vote (GOTV) budgets in the US — $2-4 billion per election cycle according to various estimates, plus billions in party advertising and SMS campaigns — turnout remains low. In off-year elections 15-25%, in presidential 60-66%. Parties spend more than ever on "informing," but the share of those forgetting about elections or not coming does not decrease.

This is an empirical admission of the failure of push-logic. No amount of reminders can force most people to remember an event that brings them nothing personally.

### 83.3. Why People Will NOT Forget after AB-EXIT

Election Day after AB-EXIT is a day when a voter PERSONALLY is entitled to something. Either $780 to their account, or their vote with triple power. In both cases, they receive something real.

An empirical analogy makes this obvious. People do not forget:
— payday;
— tax return day;
— the first day of the month when pensions or social benefits arrive;
— Black Friday and Cyber Monday;
— loan repayment day and interest receipt day on a deposit.

Thematically — people do not forget what is associated with the receipt of money to their account. This is a basic cognitive priority built into the human psyche. No agitation is needed. No billions for reminders. People put it in the calendar themselves, in advance, and discuss it with family.

Election Day after AB-EXIT falls into the same mnemonic category — as a day of receiving money. This is a qualitatively different category of event than "the day when you need to go vote."

### 83.4. Four Practical Consequences

**Consequence 1: Elimination of the GOTV industry.** Multi-billion budgets for "come vote" become unnecessary. Not because someone banned them, but because they lose sense — no one needs to be mobilized; everyone already knows and remembers. This frees up $2-4 billion in the US (according to various estimates) for each cycle, plus proportional amounts in other democracies. This money ceases to be burned on informational noise.

**Consequence 2: Elimination of the advantage of rich parties in mobilization.** Currently, a party with a large budget can "buy" more turnout through massive advertising and targeted campaigns. After AB-EXIT, this advantage disappears. Basic information about the existence of elections is already with all voters — it cannot be bought. The competition of parties switches from "who reminded loudest" to "who has the better program," which is normal democracy.

**Consequence 3: Leveling turnout between types of elections.** Currently, off-year elections (mayors, city councils, school boards) suffer from low turnout precisely because they are forgotten. This gives an advantage to party machines mobilizing a loyal base. After AB-EXIT, every election is a dividend day. Off-year turnout structurally rises to the federal level, and off-year ceases to be the "kingdom of machines." Local politics becomes as meaningful as national.

**Consequence 4: Transition from passive waiting to active planning.** People think in advance about which option to choose, discuss it with family, and make calculations (Section 80.13 on metrics that become alive). This is not a passive "oh, I should go vote" on election day, but active work for weeks and months before. This qualitatively increases the awareness of each choice and expands the temporary window of involvement from one day to several weeks.

This section additionally confirms Section 82.5 about Plurality as a superstructure over AB-EXIT. Plurality tries to make discussion more meaningful through Polis, quadratic voting, and deliberative platforms. But all this works only if people have come to participate. Plurality does not solve the problem of ARRIVAL into the system — Tang and the vTaiwan team spend enormous efforts just to get people to enter the deliberation platform in the first place.
