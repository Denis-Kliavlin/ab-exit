## 91. AB-EXIT in the Jheringian Tradition: A Doctrinal Basis for Continental Europe

### 91.1. Purpose of the Section

Section 84 establishes Route B (launching via parliamentary scandal and private referendum) for countries without the right of citizen initiative. This is an operational path. This section adds a **doctrinal basis** for the Continental European legal tradition through the work of Rudolf von Jhering (1818-1892), the founder of sociological jurisprudence and the jurisprudence of interests.

AB-EXIT methodologically aligns with the Jheringian tradition almost entirely. This provides the project with legal depth in Germany, Switzerland, Poland, Italy, and Austria — countries of civil law with a vibrant Jheringian tradition.

### 91.2. Who is Jhering and Why He Matters

Caspar Rudolf von Jhering (1818-1892) was a German jurist and founder of the sociological and historical schools of law. His two main works are:

— **Der Kampf ums Recht** (*The Struggle for Law*, 1872) — thesis: citizens have a moral obligation to themselves and society to vigorously defend their legal rights. Law develops through the conscious assertion of interests.

— **Der Zweck im Recht** / *Law as a Means to an End* (1877-1883) — central thesis: "**purpose is the creator of the entire law**" (*Zweck ist der Schöpfer des ganzen Rechts*). The law must serve public interests and not exist as a mere formality.

In modern Germany, there is the **Rudolf-von-Jhering-Institut** at the Faculty of Law of the University of Giessen. His school lives on through modern German and Continental European legal traditions.

The evolution of his thought is instructive: he began as a proponent of *Begriffsjurisprudenz* (formal positivism), then exposed its flaws himself in the 1860s. By 1872, he had finally transitioned to *Zweckjurisprudenz* (jurisprudence of purpose).

### 91.3. Five Methodological Overlaps Between AB-EXIT and Jhering’s Principles

**First — "Purpose is the creator of the entire law."** AB-EXIT is built for an explicit purpose (improving the quality of democracy through the concentration of the active electorate). Not an abstract norm, but a tool for solving a specific social problem. This is a direct application of the Jheringian principle.

**Second — Jurisprudence of interests.** AB-EXIT explicitly balances the interests of different groups:

— **The Apathetic Citizen** receives money (Section 6 of the concept).
— **The Active Citizen** receives an amplified voice (Section 6).
— **Business** receives transparency (Section 80.7).
— **The State** receives legitimacy.
— **Minorities** receive equal conditions (Section 80.10).
— **Honest Politicians** receive the structural opportunity to win through complex programs, without the need to lie, applying long-term planning and complex structural solutions instead of "take and redistribute."

Each interest is expressed explicitly and balanced through a single mechanism. This is a direct embodiment of his theory. And the sixth category is particularly important because technocrat-reformers, government experts, and business leaders with political ambitions are the natural messengers of AB-EXIT (Section 70, Portrait of the Messenger). They receive from AB-EXIT not an abstract good, but a concrete structural tool for their own victory.

**Third — A sociological approach instead of formal positivism.** AB-EXIT is built on empirical data: real voter turnout, real motivation (Pragmatists and Ideologues), real historical precedents (Norway), and an empirical comparison with ten other reforms. This is methodologically correct by Jheringian standards.

**Fourth — "Der Kampf ums Recht" — the struggle for law.** Jhering argued that citizens have a moral obligation to vigorously defend their rights. AB-EXIT gives citizens a new right — the right to choose between political participation and compensation. Those who fight for the adoption of this right through a ballot initiative (Route A) or a parliamentary scandal (Route B) act exactly in the spirit of Jhering.

**Fifth — Law above the struggle.** Jhering emphasized a nuance: even though citizens fight for rights, the law itself sits above this struggle and is not subordinated to their conflicting interests. AB-EXIT does not replace democracy, does not cancel voting, and does not destroy the institution. It creates an institutional superstructure that remains above the political struggle as a mechanism. AB-EXIT is not relativistic — it creates a fixed system that works identically for everyone.

### 91.4. Three Possible Objections from Jhering and Counterarguments

**Objection one — the problem of "exchanging a right for money."** In Jhering's understanding, the right to vote is a civic duty, not a commodity. AB-EXIT encourages the abandonment of the struggle for the right in exchange for money. This contradicts his central thesis.

The counterargument is found in his own methodology. Jhering himself said that "purpose is the creator of law." If we look not at the form (exchanging a right for money) but at the purpose (improving the quality of democracy), then the form is legitimate — it is subordinated to the purpose. If AB-EXIT empirically improves democracy (Section 80.9 Norway, Section 85 comparative analysis), then its form is methodologically valid by Jheringian standards. The purpose justifies the form if the purpose is empirically achieved.

**Objection two — the optionality of civic duty.** AB-EXIT makes the struggle for the right optional. This potentially undermines the moral foundation of citizenship.

Counterargument: AB-EXIT does not forbid the struggle; it intensifies it for those who remain. Those voting with a weight of ×2 are precisely those who "vigorously struggle for rights" in Jhering's sense. They receive double weight specifically because they refused compensation in favor of active participation. Those who take the money are not struggling against the right — they are exiting the electoral arena, leaving it to the fighters. This is a purification of the struggle for law, not its weakening.

**Objection three — the evolution of law through the struggle of interests.** If you remove the apathetic from the electoral arena, will the spectrum of interests in politics not decrease?

Counterargument: the apathetic do not represent their interests in politics now — they ignore them. AB-EXIT, for the first time, gives them economic representation (via the Civic Dividend). Their interest ("I want money instead of politics") is now institutionalized. This is an expansion of the spectrum of represented interests, not a contraction.

### 91.5. What Jhering DID NOT Find During His Lifetime — A Critical Observation

Jhering lived for 74 years and went through several intellectual evolutions. However, he **described the disease and the general approach without creating a specific institutional mechanism**.

Specifically missing from his legacy:
— An institutional mechanism for balancing interests in practice.
— An electoral system that ensures the legitimacy of purpose-driven law.
— A method for concentrating an active citizenship capable of a jurisprudence of interests.
— A structural mechanism for defense against formalism in the political plane.

For 130+ years after his death, the concrete mechanism remained an open question. His legacy — the school of the jurisprudence of interests (Philipp Heck, Roscoe Pound) — continued theoretical work, but still in the realm of theory, not institutional mechanisms.

One academic analysis calls Jhering’s case "tragic and instructive" — despite his enormous influence on subsequent jurists, he did not create a working institutional system himself.

**AB-EXIT is the first concrete institutional mechanism to realize Jheringian principles.** It is not an alternative to Jhering’s work, but its completion. His unfinished theoretical program finds a concrete institutional embodiment 134 years after his death.

## 92. Structural Property of AB-EXIT: A Paradigm Shift and the Impossibility of Criticism Through Old Theories

### 92.1. Purpose of the Section

During the work on the project, several reconstructions were made of the positions of major thinkers regarding AB-EXIT: Anne Applebaum, Sergei Guriev, Ray Dalio, Grigory Yudin, and Rudolf von Jhering. Each reconstruction revealed the same pattern, which deserves explicit recording as a structural property of the project.

**The Pattern:** Objections from scholars against AB-EXIT, derived from their public positions, are systematically refuted by their own methodology when the AB-EXIT document is analyzed. Their criticism structurally transforms into an argument FOR AB-EXIT.

This is not a coincidence. It is a structural consequence of the fact that AB-EXIT represents a paradigm shift (Section 82), rather than a reform within the existing paradigm.

### 92.2. Empirical Confirmation of the Pattern

**Applebaum.** Described the problem: democracies are degrading from within through the normalization of minor violations and kleptocracy. An expected objection: "AB-EXIT destroys the ritual significance of voting." Refuted by her own work — she documented that ritual significance has already been destroyed by populists. AB-EXIT does not destroy, but addresses what is already broken.

**Dalio.** Described the problem: fiscal crises require political coordination that does not exist. An expected objection: "AB-EXIT is a slow reform; fast hard measures are needed." Refuted by his own empirics — an analysis of 48 crises shows that the same crises yield different results depending on political quality. AB-EXIT improves political quality, which is a prerequisite for his own fiscal solutions.

**Guriev and Yudin.** Described the problem: populism and spin-dictators manipulate apathetic voters. An expected objection: "AB-EXIT can be used by authoritarian regimes against citizens." Refuted by their own analysis — apathetic voters are already an instrument of authoritarian manipulation. AB-EXIT removes them from the field, depriving spin-dictators of their primary tool.

**Jhering.** Described the problem: law must serve purposes, but formalism hinders this. An expected objection: "AB-EXIT turns a right into a commodity." Refuted by his own thesis "purpose is the creator of law." If the goal of improving democracy is achieved, the form of exchange is legitimate according to his own methodology.

In each case, the scholar's diagnosis becomes a justification for AB-EXIT, rather than a criticism of it.

### 92.3. Mechanism of the Pattern

This pattern has a structural explanation. Existing theories are formulated to **analyze the existing reality** — to describe, explain, and criticize what is. Each theory works within the framework of its own paradigm.

AB-EXIT creates a **new reality** that did not exist when these theories were formulated. This means the following:

— Scholars' objections are built on the problems of the old reality.
— AB-EXIT solves these problems through structural reorganization.
— Therefore, criticism based on old problems automatically becomes an argument FOR AB-EXIT.

Each scholar described their part of the "disease of democracy." AB-EXIT is the "cure" for this disease. Criticizing the cure from the perspective of describing the disease is structurally impossible — it only shows that AB-EXIT addresses precisely the problem that the scholar considered important.

### 92.4. Historical Analogy — Other Paradigm Shifts

A similar pattern is observed in the history of other paradigm shifts:

— **Introduction of Social Security in 1935.** Critics predicted the destruction of "American character through state dependence." Their criticism was built on the problem of elderly poverty, which was real. Social Security solved this problem, and the criticism became retrospectively irrelevant.

— **The NHS in the UK in 1948.** The British Medical Association predicted the "destruction of the doctor's independence." Their criticism was built on the problem of unequal access to medicine, which was real. The NHS solved this problem.

— **The Abolition of Slavery.** Defenders of slavery predicted the "economic collapse of the South." Their criticism was built on the economic dependence on slave labor, which was real. Abolition transformed this dependence.

This is a general pattern of major institutional innovations. Critics relying on the problems of the old reality are refuted by the innovation itself, which solves those problems.

### 92.5. Where the Pattern DOES NOT Work

It is important to fix the boundary of applicability. The pattern works for critics with **concrete analytical work on democratic problems** — those who empirically described the diseases of modern politics (Applebaum, Dalio, Guriev, Yudin, Jhering).

The pattern DOES NOT work for critics with **fundamental metaphysical objections** against the very idea of a market mechanism in politics. Such critics might say: "I do not object from the standpoint of problem diagnosis. I object from the standpoint of principle — politics and the market are fundamentally incompatible, period."

This is a different class of criticism. It is not built on the analysis of existing reality; therefore, methodological refutation through diagnosis does not work. A metaphysical objection requires a metaphysical answer — and that is a different class of discussion.

Empirically, among the serious critics we have studied with concrete analytical work, the pattern matches completely. We have not encountered metaphysical critics — perhaps because serious intellectuals rarely work in a purely metaphysical mode, preferring empirical analysis.

### 92.6. What This Means for the Project Strategy

This structural property has several practical consequences.

**First — a high probability of intellectual support from serious critics.** Scholars who seriously analyze democratic problems discover, upon a detailed study of AB-EXIT, that the concept addresses precisely the problems they described. This creates a basis for transforming potential critics into allies.

**Second — the focus of communication should be on their own work.** When addressing Applebaum — quote her work on the degradation of democracy. When addressing Dalio — quote his work on the Big Cycle. When addressing Jhering through his successors — quote "purpose is the creator of law." This is not a rhetorical trick; it is a structural substantiation that AB-EXIT solves the problems of their own analysis.

**Third — the main threat comes not from serious analysts, but from metaphysical opponents and partisan groups** (Section 88 — structural analysis of opponents). This changes the priorities of defense.

**Fourth — every public criticism from a serious scholar works for the project.** If a respected analyst publicly criticizes AB-EXIT from the position of their work, we can show that AB-EXIT solves that analyst's problem. This creates a public situation where the criticism reveals itself as support.

This structural property is a consequence of the paradigmatic nature of AB-EXIT (Section 82) and relies on the following sections of the document:

### 92.8. Conclusion of the Structural Observation

AB-EXIT possesses a rare property: serious criticism from the positions of existing analytical theories about democratic problems is structurally refuted by the critic's own methodology. This is a consequence of the concept’s paradigmatic nature.

This does not mean that AB-EXIT is invulnerable to criticism in principle. Metaphysical objections, political attacks from structural opponents (Section 88), technical implementation problems, and unexpected empirical data — all these remains possible. But classic academic criticism based on the diagnosis of existing problems in democracy works, in the case of AB-EXIT, as a justification rather than a refutation.

This is a structural advantage that must be understood and used carefully. Exaggeration leads to overconfidence and the loss of the ability to hear criticism. Correct use is the calm knowledge that a serious dialogue with serious critics does not pose an existential threat to the project, as the structure of their methodology works for the project, not against it.

## 93. Honest Politicians as Key Beneficiaries of AB-EXIT: Empirically Confirmed Structural Pressure and Its Overcoming

### 93.1. Purpose of the Section

Section 91.3 records six categories of interests balanced by AB-EXIT. The sixth category — honest politicians — deserves a separate expanded analysis because it structurally changes the positioning of the entire project.

Prior to this section, AB-EXIT was presented as a reform against the influence of "bad politicians" (populists, manipulators, spin-dictators). This is true, but it is not the whole truth. The whole truth: AB-EXIT is a **reform for honest politicians**, giving them the structural opportunity to win without the need to simplify their program to the level of populist rhetoric.

This is a qualitative shift in positioning. Not a "reform against the political class," but a "reform for that class of politicians who truly want to serve society but face structural pressure that empirically reduces their chances of victory."

**Additional structural observation — AB-EXIT addresses the voter as an adult.** The modern electoral system structurally requires politicians to treat voters as children — simplifying, hiding complexities, manipulating emotions instead of arguments, and promising the impossible. This demeaning treatment is built into the very structure of the system. AB-EXIT is the first mass political proposal that treats the voter as an adult capable of a rational choice based on real data. This aspect of the concept is expanded separately in subsections 93.9 and 93.10.

**Methodological Note.** The original version of this section (version 6.50) contained the categorical formulation "an honest politician is forced either to lie or to lose," presented as a structural fact without empirical verification. After a request from the concept's author for a scientific basis for the thesis, verification was conducted through peer-reviewed studies. The results showed that the main line of thought is empirically confirmed (Janezic & Gallego, PNAS 2020), but categorical formulations require softening to probabilistic ones. This section reflects the result of the verification. This is an illustration of a general methodological principle: strong assertions must rely on empirical research, not on intuitive generalizations.

### 93.2. Two Fundamentally Different Categories of Politicians and Empirical Confirmation

Any serious analysis of politics must recognize that politicians are not homogeneous. There exist at least two fundamentally different categories — or rather, a spectrum between them:

**Category 1 — politicians who use democratic procedures for personal power and enrichment.** Their tools: mobilizing the apathetic, manipulating emotions, populist rhetoric, promises to "take and redistribute," and simple answers to complex questions.

**Category 2 — politicians who sincerely wish to help their country and people.** They want to apply complex structural solutions: fiscal discipline, long-term planning, infrastructure investments, educational reforms, and economic innovations.

**Empirical Confirmation of the Existence of Structural Pressure:**

The thesis of structural pressure in modern campaigns is confirmed by peer-reviewed studies.

The main empirical confirmation is the study by Janezic and Gallego, published in *PNAS* (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*) in 2020. Using a sample of 816 elected politicians in Spain, the authors used behavioral games to measure preferences for truthfulness and found: "politicians who prefer telling the truth have lower reelection rates, suggesting that honesty may not pay in politics." This is the first scientific empirical confirmation that structural pressure against honesty exists in modern democracies.

Additional studies show the mechanisms of this pressure:

— A 2023 study in the *Journal of Economic Behavior* showed that "promises that are too generous to be credible in transparent institutions become credible in opaque environments. Competition leads to more generous promises in opaque institutions than in transparent ones." That is, in modern opaque campaigns, candidates are structurally pressured toward exaggeration.

— A 2021 study in *Political Behavior* established a "truth bias" in voters: "people are more likely to judge statements as true than as false, even in a political context where voters might have been more skeptical." This means that lies work in the short term because voters are poor at identifying them.

**Critical Nuances to be Recorded Honestly:**

Structural pressure is a **probabilistic effect**, not an absolute inevitability. Honest politicians have lower chances of reelection, but not zero. The categorical "either lie or lose" is a simplification of reality.

Significant variation exists among politicians. The PNAS 2020 study explicitly notes: "some have a robust preference for telling the truth, even when lying is personally beneficial and not observed by others." These politicians exist and win, but less frequently than their less principled colleagues.

The categorization into "Category 1 / Category 2" is a simplification for analysis; in reality, a spectrum exists between these poles. AB-EXIT works for the entire spectrum, not just the extremes.

**What This Means Structurally:**

Even the most honest politician in the existing system finds themselves in a dilemma: either simplify the program to a level understandable by the apathetic mass (losing content) or lose. This is not a moral criticism of individuals — it is a structural requirement of the system.

This is not a choice between "lie or lose" in its pure form. It is a structural pressure toward **simplification, exaggeration, and short-term rhetoric**, which reduces the effectiveness of the honest politician's work. And it is empirically confirmed that this pressure reduces reelection chances (PNAS 2020).

### 93.3. Illustrative Examples of Structural Pressure on Honest Politicians

These examples are illustrations of the described mechanism, not proof. The proof is provided by the PNAS 2020 study of 816 politicians. Each individual case may have multiple reasons for failure; their significance lies in their alignment with the general empirically recorded pattern.

### 95.2. Downs Paradox (1957) — The Fundamental Theoretical Basis of the Problem

Anthony Downs, in his work *An Economic Theory of Democracy* (1957), formulated what later became known as the Downs Paradox or the Paradox of Voting. The essence: a rational voter compares the benefit of voting (which arises only if they are pivotal) with the fixed cost of participation (time, travel, information costs). In large electorates, the probability of being pivotal is so small that the expected benefit almost never exceeds the cost.

From this follows a fundamental theoretical result: for a rational self-interested voter in a large democracy, abstention should be the dominant strategy.

The Downs Paradox spawned a 70-year academic tradition (Riker-Ordeshook 1968, Aldrich 1993, Feddersen 2004, Geys 2006, and hundreds of other works) attempting to explain why people still vote despite rational impracticality.

Possible explanations considered in the literature:

— Voters act not as rational actors, but under the influence of social norms.
— Voting as an expressive action (expression of identity, not an instrumental action).
— Altruistic theory of voting (concern for others).
— Insurance against catastrophe (Downs himself proposed this version in Chapter 14).
— Mixed motivations including duty, identity, and social pressure.

All these explanations converge on one point: the current system of participation in democracy rests **not on rational incentives, but on irrational factors** — morality, identity, and cultural pressure.

**Key conclusion for AB-EXIT.** A 70-year theoretical tradition formally proves that the current system of participation in democracy **structurally contradicts the rational behavior of the voter**. AB-EXIT is the first institutional innovation that solves the problem rationally: it provides economic compensation to those for whom participation is economically impractical and amplifies the voice of those for whom participation has real instrumental value.

AB-EXIT does not contradict the Downs Paradox — it solves it institutionally through mechanism design.

### 95.3. Buchanan-Tullock (1962) — An Explicit Description of the Problem AB-EXIT Solves

James Buchanan (Nobel Laureate 1986) and Gordon Tullock, in their work *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy* (1962), explicitly formulate the problem that AB-EXIT institutionally solves.

A direct quote from Chapter 11, "Simple Majority Voting and the Theory of Games":

"An individual participant in collective decision-making may, in many of the real choices made through the political process, prefer to 'get out of the game.' This does not mean that the individual necessarily wants to withdraw from participation in the whole set of games represented by governmental activity (although conceptually he might want this also). In any case, the individual normally can neither choose the political 'games' in which he wants to participate nor withdraw from the ultimate social... He must remain a participant in every question with which the group is faced. ... However, in political groups such action is normally impossible."

This is a critically important quote. Buchanan and Tullock explicitly stated in 1962:

1. An individual may rationally prefer to exit the political game.
2. In political groups, this is structurally impossible.
3. This creates a fundamental problem of legitimacy for collective decisions.

**AB-EXIT is the first formal institutional solution to the problem identified by the Nobel Laureate 64 years ago.** The concept provides exactly the exit mechanism from the political game that Buchanan and Tullock recognized as impossible in their time.

This provides AB-EXIT with powerful academic legitimation: the concept does not contradict classical public choice theory (Buchanan-Tullock); it **completes** it through institutional innovation.

### 95.4. Mechanism Design Framework and Participation Constraint

Mechanism design is a branch of game theory developed by Leonid Hurwicz (Nobel 2007), Eric Maskin (Nobel 2007), and Roger Myerson (Nobel 2007). Mechanism design approaches the design of games in reverse: they start with a desired result (social choice function) and then work backward to find the game (mechanism) that realizes this result.

AB-EXIT methodologically is exactly a mechanism design approach. It begins with the desired result (the electorate consists of Pragmatists and Ideologues capable of rational choice) and works in the opposite direction to find a mechanism that realizes this result through a bilateral choice (compensation or an amplified voice).

**Participation Constraint (individual rationality constraint).** A central concept of mechanism design. A mechanism must leave all participants at least as well off as they would be without participation. Three types of participation constraints exist:

1. Ex-post (the strongest form): knowing the types of all others, the agent decides to participate.
2. Interim: knowing their own type, the agent decides to participate, expecting utility to be higher than the outside option.
3. Ex-ante (the weakest form): without knowing types, the agent decides to participate based on the prior distribution.

**AB-EXIT strictly satisfies the participation constraint of all three types:**

— **Ex-post.** Knowing their decision (to take the Civic Dividend or vote), the agent receives a result better than without AB-EXIT (where they either vote against their rationality or abstain without compensation).

— **Interim.** Knowing their preferences (Pragmatist with calculations, Ideologue with values, apathetic with other priorities), the agent receives an expected utility higher than the outside option.

— **Ex-ante.** Without knowing their exact type, the agent receives a positive expected utility because, in any case, AB-EXIT gives them an option better than the status quo.

This is a formal substantiation of AB-EXIT's legitimacy within the framework of mechanism design theory. The concept doesn't just "look fair" — it mathematically satisfies the strict criterion of the participation constraint.

### 95.5. Gibbard-Satterthwaite Theorem — Why AB-EXIT Circumvents the Fundamental Negative Result

The Gibbard-Satterthwaite Theorem (Gibbard 1973, Satterthwaite 1975) is a fundamental negative result in social choice theory. The theorem states: any non-dictatorial voting rule with three or more alternatives is vulnerable to strategic voting (tactical voting, not for one's real preferences).

From this follows the fundamental result that there is no "fair" voting system without an incentive for manipulation. This is a frightening result — it seems to make the design of a fair voting system impossible.

Mechanism design was partially developed as a way to escape Gibbard-Satterthwaite. Noam Nisan explicitly characterizes this as an escape route: expand the class of mechanisms considered, allow probabilistic mechanisms, limit the domain of preferences, and so on.

**AB-EXIT represents a new escape route** not described in existing literature:

The concept circumvents Gibbard-Satterthwaite not through manipulation of voting mechanics, but through an **opt-out mechanism with compensation**. If a voter does not want to participate in strategic voting, they can legally exit the system for a Civic Dividend. This is a qualitatively different approach.

ВIn an ordinary voting system, a voter faces a choice: vote honestly (risking a loss) or tactically (violating their own preferences). AB-EXIT adds a third option: receive compensation and not participate in this round at all.

Those who remain to vote do so because the instrumental value of participation for them exceeds the Civic Dividend. This reduces the incentive for strategic voting: if you preferred tactical manipulation, you could simply take the Civic Dividend. Those who stay are those for whom voting has real value, not a tactical game.

This is a new theoretical result deserving formal development in the form of an academic paper.

### 95.6. Vote Buying Theory — A Related but Opposite Paradigm

Vote Buying Theory (Dekel-Jackson-Wolinsky 2004, Dekel-Wolinsky 2003, and later) is the formal analysis of situations where parties or interest groups buy the votes of voters or legislators. There is a rich literature (Tullock 1972, Snyder 1991, Groseclose-Snyder 1996, Eguia-Xefteris 2019, and hundreds of other works).

From the work of Dekel-Jackson-Wolinsky (2004): "We investigate the consequences of vote buying, assuming that this practice is permitted and free of stigma. Two parties competing in binary elections can buy votes through a sequential game of bidding with the help of up-front payments and/or campaign promises."

Key result: depending on the structure (up-front payments vs. campaign promises, budget constraints, voter preferences), parties can effectively buy elections. The result can be Pareto-inefficient.

**AB-EXIT is an opposite paradigm across several critical parameters:**

— **Who pays.** In Vote Buying Theory, parties or interest groups pay. In AB-EXIT, the state pays as a neutral actor.

— **What is paid for.** In Vote Buying Theory, payment is for a vote for a specific candidate or policy. In AB-EXIT, payment is for exiting the voting regardless of who wins.

— **Who benefits.** Vote Buying benefits one side (the buyer) and is disadvantageous to others. AB-EXIT is beneficial to all participants within the participation constraint.

— **Long-term effect.** Vote Buying distorts elections and concentrates political power in the hands of resource owners. AB-EXIT, conversely, purifies the electorate from manipulations and concentrates political influence in the hands of Pragmatists and Ideologues.

**This provides AB-EXIT with a powerful rhetorical tool in academic discussion:**

Any attempt to equate AB-EXIT to vote buying is formally incorrect. Conceptually, these are opposite paradigms. AB-EXIT does not buy votes for an outcome — it compensates a right regardless of the outcome. This is a critical distinction that must be explicitly formulated in academic presentations.

### 95.8. Shapley Value for the Formal Mathematical Basis of "Vote ×2 on Average"

The Shapley Value (Lloyd Shapley, Nobel 2012) is a formal mathematical tool for evaluating an agent's power in a cooperative game or voting system. The voting power index of Shapley takes into account all permutations of the voting order and assigns power to the voter who "tips" the majority.

Algorithm: count how many times each agent is pivotal in all possible sequences and divide by the total number of sequences. This yields voting power as a proportion of pivotal moments.

**Application to AB-EXIT.** When part of the electorate exits for a Civic Dividend, the Shapley Value of each remaining voter indeed grows. This is not a rhetorical "vote ×2"; it is a mathematically calculated value.

A simple example. If 50% of the electorate exits, the remaining 50% receive double weight on average. This is intuitively obvious. But the Shapley Value allows for precise calculation for any outcome distribution:

— If 40% exit, the remaining ones receive a Shapley Value × 1.67 (1/0.6).
— If 50% exit, × 2.0.
— If 60% exit, × 2.5.
— If 70% exit, × 3.33.

These values can be strictly formalized and proven for any voting rule. This provides AB-EXIT with a mathematical base that can be presented in an academic publication, defended in peer-review, and used in any serious theoretical discussion.

An additional nuance: the Shapley Value also allows for taking into account the **heterogeneity of preferences** of the remaining voters. If Pragmatists and Ideologues have different distributions of preferences, their Shapley Values may differ. This provides a tool for fine analysis of exactly how the political influence of different groups will change after AB-EXIT.
