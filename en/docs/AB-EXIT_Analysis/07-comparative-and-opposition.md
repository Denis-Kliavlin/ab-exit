## 84. Route B: Launch via Parliamentary Scandal and Private Referendum for Countries without the Right of Citizen Initiative

### 84.1. Purpose of the Section

Section 68 describes the route for launching AB-EXIT via a citizen-initiated statute — submitting an initiative directly to a referendum, bypassing parliament. This route works in the USA (several states with the right of initiative), Switzerland, and Italy. However, a large part of the world — Poland, Hungary, Germany, France, Great Britain, Japan, Russia, and dozens of other countries — does not have the right of a citizen-initiated statute. Citizens there physically cannot bring a question to a referendum outside of parliament.

This is a serious flaw in the launch route, which any competent critic with experience in Eastern Europe (the typical position of Anne Applebaum) would correctly identify. This section closes that flaw through an alternative mechanism that does not require an institutional right of initiative.

### 84.2. Mechanism of Route B

The alternative route consists of two parallel actions that reinforce each other:

**First — an underdog politician publicly introduces AB-EXIT into the parliamentary agenda.** This only requires one deputy with the readiness to submit a bill. The deputy can be from any faction, including marginal or new parties. The goal here is not to pass the law (the probability is close to zero in authoritarianizing systems), but to create public discussion and a media event. Submitting a bill is a public act that the media cannot completely ignore.

**Second — a private referendum is conducted in parallel.** This is not an election, not a legal act, but a massive verified poll organized by civil society. It can be conducted via a special application with identity verification, through signature campaigns with notary certification, or via stationary voting points organized in public spaces. Such a referendum has no legal force, but its political power is immense — it shows the real distribution of citizens' opinions, which cannot be contested.

These two actions work together. Parliament ignores the bill? The private referendum shows mass support, and parliament's ignorance becomes a scandal. The private referendum is declared a fake? The deputy in parliament publicly defends it and demands an official vote. Each action reinforces the other.

### 84.3. Four Scenarios of Government Reaction and Why Each is a Losing One

Parliament and the executive branch find themselves facing a choice of four options, and in each one, they lose legitimacy.

**Option 1 — silence and ignore.** This is an attempt to show that the question is unserious. But as soon as the private referendum collects a significant number of signatures or shows mass support — parliament's silence becomes a weapon against itself. "They do not even react to a million citizens who demanded a discussion." This is ideal fuel for an underdog's campaign. Every day of silence strengthens their position.

**Option 2 — mock and reject.** "This is populism, this is a utopia, this is unfeasible." Here, the government falls into a trap. If you call the proposal of a million citizens a "utopia," you are calling them fools. And they are a mobilized electorate that will remember this. Hillary Clinton's precedent with the "deplorables" in 2016 showed that this mistake can be decisive.

Additionally, in rejecting it, parliament must EXPLAIN why. Any substantive explanation drags them into debates on the merits of AB-EXIT, where the government has no arguments (Section 76 — three answers to critics makes their position inherently a losing one). Any non-substantive explanation humiliates a million supporters.

**Option 3 — legally ban the private referendum.** The most interesting scenario, because it reveals a fundamental contradiction. A private referendum is simply a poll. It is a form of freedom of speech, association, and assembly. To ban it, a law would have to be introduced forbidding citizens from knowing each other's opinions. This is legislatively impossible in any country claiming to be a democracy.

A government that attempts this immediately turns into an obviously authoritarian one in the eyes of the international community. Poland, Germany, and France cannot do this because of the European Court of Human Rights, the EU, NATO, and the international press. Any attempt at a ban becomes powerful evidence that AB-EXIT threatens not democracy, but specifically THEIR power.

Even in more autocratic systems (Russia, Hungary, Turkey), a total ban is impossible — one can persecute the organizers, but a poll via an encrypted application with international verification is technically impossible to stop. And every instance of persecution becomes an international scandal, legitimizing AB-EXIT outside the country.

**Option 4 — discuss seriously in parliament.** Paradoxically, the best option for the government — but they rarely choose it. Serious discussion legitimizes AB-EXIT as an idea but allows the process to be stretched out through commissions, expert assessments, and hearings for years. During this time, the idea may lose momentum.

However, parliamentary systems organically resist any serious reforms that threaten the composition of the parliament itself. Deputies know that AB-EXIT will remove their base — apathetic voters. They choose options 1, 2, or 3 and lose in each one.

Thus, the government has no winning strategy for a reaction. This is a classic political trap for an authoritarianizing system.

### 84.4. Historical Precedents for the Success of Such a Mechanism

Route B is not theoretical — it is an empirically functioning mechanism that has been used many times in history to create unremovable pressure on power.

**Sunflower Movement (Taiwan, 2014).** 500,000 people in the streets + even more online, occupying parliament for 24 days on live air. The government could neither ignore, nor ban, nor negotiate. The result was the rejection of the imposed trade agreement with the PRC and the legitimization of g0v as a civic technological movement. Taiwanese democracy was strengthened through this event.

**Solidarność (Poland, 1980-1989).** A trade union as a parallel power structure. The government of the PPR could not recognize it (this would be an admission of the defeat of communism), could not destroy it (repression created an international scandal), and could not negotiate (any concessions strengthened Solidarność). The result was the Round Table of 1989 and the transition to democracy.

**Hong Kong (2019).** 2 million people in the streets in a city with a population of 7 million. The principle of "be water" — a fluid, decentralized mobilization. Beijing's power lost legitimacy globally, even when it suppressed the movement by force. Hong Kong as a financial center lost decades of development.

**Belarus (2020).** Hundreds of thousands in the streets after the falsification of elections. Lukashenko held onto power but lost legitimacy irreversibly. Currently, Belarus exists only due to Russian patronage; any change in circumstances will lead to a change in regime.

Not all of these movements immediately led to a change in regime. But they all created unremovable pressure, changed the balance of power, and in the long term, work against authoritarianizing power. AB-EXIT via Route B utilizes the same class of mechanisms.

### 84.5. Technical Requirements for a Private Referendum

For a private referendum to have political weight, it must be verifiable. This implies several technical requirements:

— **Verification of the identity of voters.** Not an anonymous poll "on the internet," but the confirmed participation of real citizens. Technically, this is solved through bank identification, notary certification of signatures, and physical voting points with document verification.

— **Protection against double voting.** Each citizen can vote only once. This is solved through a distributed ledger (Section 78 — the technical architecture works here as well).

— **Public verification of results.** Anyone can verify the results independently. Open code, open data, international observers.

— **Protection against hacking and tampering.** Cryptographic protection of voting data. This is an established industry using the same approaches as financial blockchain systems.

All these requirements are achievable with existing technologies. The specific implementation is a task for working groups at the implementation stage (Section 78.9 — parameters for technical teams).

### 84.6. Legal Protection of a Private Referendum

A private referendum cannot be cleanly banned because it relies on three fundamental rights protected in any democracy and most authoritarian systems:

— **Freedom of speech.** The right of citizens to express their opinions on political issues.
— **Freedom of association.** The right to unite for collective action.
— **Freedom of assembly.** The right to hold public events.

Polling citizens on a political issue is a direct implementation of these three rights. Any ban on the poll violates all three simultaneously. This creates legal and moral protection that an authoritarian government finds difficult to bypass without an open violation of its own constitution.

Additionally, a private referendum does not claim legal force. It is a poll, not an election. It does not require state registration, does not issue binding decisions, and does not replace institutions. It is simply a way to learn the opinion of citizens. A ban on such a poll cannot be justified by the protection of institutions — on the contrary, a ban shows that the government fears the opinion of the citizens.

### 84.7. Where This Works Especially Well and Where It is More Difficult

**Where Route B works well:**

— Democracies with problems (Poland under PiS, Hungary under Orbán, Turkey). Power is authoritarianizing, but institutional forms of democracy are preserved. Route B uses these forms against the power itself.

— Mature parliamentary democracies (Germany, France, Great Britain, Japan, Canada, Australia). Here, resistance will be softer (Option 4 is more likely), but the media channel is wider, and pressure can be created through major media outlets.

— Democracies with participation problems (Italy, Spain, Brazil). Route B mobilizes exactly those who are disillusioned, which increases the chances of success.

**Where Route B is more difficult:**

— Closed authoritarian systems (Russia, China, Iran). Technically, conducting a private referendum is more difficult, and organizers are subject to repression. But even here, work is possible through the diaspora and international organization of polls with verification.

— Small autocracies under patronage (Belarus, Venezuela, Central Asian regimes). Depends on the readiness of the patron (Russia, Cuba) to support repression. In moments of the patron's weakening, windows for launch are possible.

— Hybrid regimes (Serbia, Hong Kong before 2020, Turkey). Depends on specific circumstances and the rhetoric of the moment.

### 84.8. Connection with Section 68 — Two Parallel Routes

After the addition of this section, the project has TWO parallel launch routes:

**Route A (Section 68): citizen-initiated statute.** Applicable in the USA (several states), Switzerland, and Italy. Direct bypassing of parliament through citizen initiative. The launch route is Oregon 2028 as the first real window.

**Route B (Section 84): parliamentary scandal plus private referendum.** Applicable in most other democracies and hybrid systems of the world. An attack on parliament from the outside through public pressure. The launch route is any country where there is a ready underdog politician and a functioning civil society.

These routes do not compete; they complement each other. Success in one country via either route creates a precedent for spreading via the other route in other countries. If Oregon passes via Route A, this creates legitimization for Poland via Route B, and so on.

Paradoxically, Route B may turn out to be more effective than Route A in the long term because it works by creating mass public support that survives even without a legal victory. An Oregon referendum is a binary event (win or lose). A Polish parliamentary scandal with a private referendum is a multi-year process that changes political culture even if a specific law is not immediately passed.

### 84.9. Conclusion

AB-EXIT is not tied to a single type of political system. It has two independent launch routes for two different classes of countries. This removes the last structural limitation on the concept's applicability and makes it universally functional for the entire world, except for the most closed authoritarian systems (where no reform is possible without a regime change for other reasons).

This is a critical expansion of the project. After the addition of this section, AB-EXIT ceases to be a "reform for the USA via Oregon" and becomes a "global concept with two adaptable implementation routes."

## 85. Comparative Analysis of 10 Similar Ideas from the Last 20 Years: What Delivers Results

### 85.1. Purpose of the Section

This section examines 10 ideas that appeared over the last 20 years (2005-2026) and fall into the category of "reforms of democratic mechanics with a possible economic element." The goal is not to describe the ideas but to extract stable patterns of success and failure so that AB-EXIT can rely on the empirical experience of its predecessors rather than being built from scratch.

The section also records one specific observation: AB-EXIT surpasses the closest ideologically related idea (Plurality / quadratic voting) in terms of communication clarity and the presence of a ready implementation mechanism.

### 85.2. Ten Ideas with Analysis

**1. Ranked Choice Voting (RCV).** Adopted in San Francisco in 2004, in Maine in 2016 (via initiative), and in Alaska in 2020. By 2026, 7 states have RCV, while 19 states have banned it by law. *What worked:* the centralized advocacy organization FairVote with 30+ years of expertise, the "local pilots → states" strategy, anchor donors, and an anti-party narrative. *What did not work:* partisan blocking in red states, and the complexity of explaining the mechanics to the average voter. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* local pilots create empirical proof before a statewide launch.

**2. UBI / Yang's Freedom Dividend.** Yang launched his campaign in 2017 around $1000/month. He personally paid several families from campaign funds in 2019. By 2026, UBI has not been adopted at the federal level, but pilots exist in dozens of cities (Stockton was the first). *What worked:* a bright, simple message, a candidate-evangelist as the face of the idea, empirical demonstration through real payments, and cross-party appeal. *What did not work:* a lack of a realistic fiscal model for scaling; the idea got stuck at the local level. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* a human face for the idea makes it recognizable, but without a fiscal model, it remains local.

**3. Citizens' Assemblies / Sortition.** Ireland 2012-18 (abortion referendum via assembly, success), France 2019-20 (climate convention). By 2026, there are dozens of assemblies worldwide. *What worked:* the bingo signal that "ordinary people can," parliamentary approval in Ireland, and specific cases with measurable results. *What did not work:* assemblies provide recommendations and do not have legislative power; parliament can emasculate them (Macron with the French proposals). *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* partnership with parliament provides legitimacy, but if parliament retains the final word, the idea is easily diluted. This is an argument for citizen initiative (Route A, Section 68).

**4. Quadratic Voting / Plurality (RadicalxChange, Glen Weyl).** The book *Radical Markets* 2018, the RxC Foundation 2018, use in the Colorado House 2019 and Gitcoin Grants. By 2026, it is academically known, but there is no governmental implementation. *What worked:* intellectual elegance attracted Buterin, Tang, and academic circles from Microsoft Research, Harvard, and MIT; the open-source book *Plurality* (2024) was distributed for free; a strong theoretical base. *What did not work:* the concept is too complex for the general public, "quadratic" requires an explanation of mathematics, there is no bright message, and usage remained in niche contexts. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* intellectual elegance ≠ public success.

**5. Top-Two / Top-Four Primaries.** California 2010 (Prop 14), Washington 2008, Alaska 2020 (with RCV). By 2026, it operates in 3 states. *What worked:* an alliance with anti-partisan movements, the promise to reduce extremism, and empirical support after implementation (Mary Peltola, Lisa Murkowski in Alaska). *What did not work:* parties actively block it — it is an attack on their primary monopoly. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* attacking the party monopoly works through direct elections. AB-EXIT goes further — it attacks the mobilization base of both parties (apathetics) economically.

**6. Independent Redistricting Commissions.** California 2008 and 2010 (Prop 11 and 20), Michigan 2018, Colorado, Utah, Ohio. By 2026, about 12 states have independent commissions. *What worked:* a clear anti-corruption narrative, partisan balance (both parties could be victims), anchor donors (Chan-Zuckerberg, Hewlett), and submission via ballot initiative to bypass parliament. *What did not work:* partisan resistance in red states. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* the "bypass parliament via ballot initiative" structure works (our Route A). An anti-corruption narrative attracts all sides.

**7. Vote-by-Mail.** Oregon 1998, Colorado 2013, Washington 2011, expansion after COVID-19. By 2026, 8 states have universal mail-in, while red states have introduced restrictions. *What worked:* COVID-19 as a catalyst, existing models (Oregon), and active Democratic promotion. *What did not work:* partisan polarization after 2020 (Trump-MAGA narrative about falsifications). *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* reform can become a partisan battle; it is important to avoid association with one party. AB-EXIT, through the narrative of "money for everyone," is structurally harder to partisanize.

**8. State-level UBI Experiments.** Stockton SEED 2019 ($500/month × 125 residents × 2 years), Mayors for a Guaranteed Income coalition 2020. By 2026, 100+ cities in the USA. *What worked:* empirical results of Stockton (published 2021), a mayor-to-mayor network, and association with Yang's UBI. *What did not work:* pilots remained pilots, funding was private, and scaling to a state UBI did not occur. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* local pilots with empirical data are a strong tool for legitimation.

**9. Worldcoin (Sam Altman).** Launched 2021, open launch July 2023. By 2026, tens of millions of users, but regulatory problems (Kenya, Hong Kong, Europe). *What worked:* venture funding from Altman ($240M+), global spread, and connection with the AI narrative. *What did not work:* privacy problems (biometrics as a mandatory condition), regulatory bans, and perception as a "technological utopia of billionaires." *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* avoid association with biometrics and control by a single corporation. AB-EXIT via citizen initiative and state administration is more legitimate. Minimizing technology in the public description (Section 78.10) is the right direction.

**10. Liquid Democracy (Pirate Party Germany).** The LiquidFeedback platform since 2010, 8.9% in Berlin 2011. By 2026, the Pirate Party has practically disappeared, and the tool remains an academic concept. *What worked:* intellectual attractiveness, transparency, and working technology. *What did not work:* scaling did not occur, complexity of explanation, and technical problems of the platform. *Lesson for AB-EXIT:* idea + technology is not enough. An emotional face, a simple message, and an anchor political partner are needed.

### 85.3. What Works (Common Patterns of Successful Ideas)

**First: a simple, memorable message in 5 seconds.** RCV: "rank the candidates." UBI: "$1000 for everyone." Top-Two: "the top two move on." The more complex the explanation, the worse the result. AB-EXIT: "money or an enhanced voice" — works.

**Second: local pilots before big victories.** RCV — cities first, states later. UBI — Stockton, then other cities. Anti-gerrymandering — one state at a time. Without local proof, an idea remains a theory.

**Third: anchor donor + mass base.** All successful reforms had an anchor donor (Action Now Initiative, Chan-Zuckerberg, Dorsey). A pure grassroots campaign without money does not work. A purely donor-driven campaign without a local base also does not work.

**Fourth: bypassing parliament via ballot initiative.** Every successful reform of the last 20 years passed through direct democracy, not through legislation. The parliamentary path is blocked almost every time. Validates Route A (Section 68).

**Fifth: empirical verification after the first implementation.** RCV in Maine → data → spread. Stockton SEED → published results → spread. Without measurable data after the pilot, an idea does not scale.

**Sixth: cross-partisan narrative.** Anti-gerrymandering worked because it hit both parties. AB-EXIT via Section 80.7 (works for all three camps) does this structurally.

### 85.4. What Does Not Work (Common Patterns of Failures)

— Dependence on one political party (Vote-by-mail became "Democratic" — a ceiling of support).
— Complex technical concepts without a simple explanation (Quadratic Voting, Liquid Democracy).
— Control by a single actor (Worldcoin as an Altman project).
— Lack of a first real implementation (Liquid Democracy outside the Pirate Party).

### 85.5. AB-EXIT vs. the Entire Class of Reforms — Structural Superiority via Combination of Parameters

A comparison of AB-EXIT with a single idea (for example, with Plurality) shows superiority in several aspects. However, a more accurate analysis shows that AB-EXIT surpasses the ENTIRE class of studied reforms due to a unique combination of parameters that none of the ten ideas in Section 85.2 possess.

**Five parameters by which ideas are compared:**

1. **Clarity of message** — can the essence be explained in 5 seconds without special knowledge?
2. **Material incentive for the voter** — is there a direct personal benefit from implementation?

If one looks at major social, political, and philosophical initiatives of the last hundred years, a steady pattern emerges. The name of the author or the first active popularizer fuses with the initiative and becomes an inseparable part of it in the public consciousness.

In academia: the Krugman model, Stiglitz inequality, Sen's capability approach, the Acemoglu institutions thesis. The ideas were developed collectively for decades. The name fused with the one who became the main public voice of the topic.

These are not isolated cases. It is a stable pattern in all areas where ideas reach mass implementation.

It works through a specific human mechanism. An idea in its pure form is an abstraction. For the mass consciousness to absorb it, a face is needed. This is an empirical fact of cognitive psychology: people remember people, not concepts. A concept is remembered ONLY when it has a human face.
