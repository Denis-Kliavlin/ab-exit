## 59. The Price of Poor Management: A Layered Calculation and Assessment of the Economic Effect of AB-EXIT

### 59.1. Why Calculate in Layers

A common mistake (often made out of a desire to appear cautious) is to label losses from corruption as a "respectable" underestimated figure, such as 15% of GDP. In reality, the figure is several times higher, and this only becomes apparent through a layered analysis. Each layer is real and measured separately; together, they present an honest picture.

### 59.2. Five Layers of Loss

**Layer 1. Direct Corruption (Bribes, Kickbacks, Embezzlement).** The UN and the World Bank estimate global corruption at ~5% of world GDP. However, this is a planetary average that includes Denmark and Singapore. For countries with problematic governance (Argentina, Ukraine, Russia), estimates for direct corruption are 10-15% of GDP.

**Layer 2. Inefficient Government Procurement.** The state systematically pays more than the market: a road costing $1M from a private contractor = $3M from the state. Often, this is not theft but a lack of pressure for efficiency. The OECD estimates losses in public procurement at 10-30% of its volume; public procurement accounts for 12-20% of GDP. The arithmetic: 20% × 20% = 4% of GDP lost on procurement alone.

**Layer 3. Insane Construction and Ghost Projects.** California High-Speed Rail: $128 billion, 0 km completed. Airports, bridges, and stadiums to nowhere — "white elephants." In Argentina — unfinished state enterprises from the Peron era. One-time write-offs of tens of billions; spread across years, this accounts for several percent of GDP constantly.

**Layer 4. Bankrupt Cities.** Detroit ($18-20 billion in debt). Stockton, San Bernardino, Vallejo in California. Puerto Rico ($70 billion default). Not "inefficiency at the margins" — but a total collapse of territorial management. Each bankruptcy equals decades of lost growth for millions.

**Layer 5. Lost Growth — The Largest and Most Invisible Layer.** This is not stolen money, but money that was never born due to poor management. Argentina in 1900 = wealthier than France. Argentina in 2025 = several times poorer. The difference wasn't stolen in a single instance — a country that should have grown at 3.5% for a century grew at 1.5%. Compound interest over 100 years: the difference between 3.5% and 1.5% = a ~7-fold difference in final GDP. A well-managed Argentina would be 5-7 times wealthier. Poor management didn't "consume" 15% — it consumed the vast majority of the entire economy that could have existed.

### 59.3. The Honest Figure — Separating Two Questions

One must distinguish between two different measurements:

**Current Annual Losses** (corruption + procurement inefficiency + ghost projects + bankruptcies): for a poorly managed country, 20-30% of GDP every year is realistic. Not 15%.

**Accumulated Unrealized Potential** (Layer 5): this is not a percentage of current GDP; it is a multiple. A well-managed Argentina = 300-500% of its current state. The same applies to Russia and Ukraine.

### 59.4. Assessing the Effect of AB-EXIT — The Arithmetic

If current losses = 25% of GDP per year, and AB-EXIT over a transition period (10 years) closes half of that (12.5%), distributed over 10 years = +1.25% of additional growth per year solely from eliminating current losses.

Plus, the activation of compound interest: as soon as management becomes efficient, the previously unrealized growth begins. In a country with a large gap (Argentina), +3-5% of additional growth during the transition period is realistic.

Conclusion: +2% of additional growth is a conservative LOWER bound, not an optimistic ceiling. For countries with a large historical gap, the potential is higher.

### 59.5. Three Disciplining Caveats (Rigor, Not Censorship)

To make the figure defensible before economists (for example, of the Austrian school), three conditions must be met:

First: this is a LEVEL effect, not a permanent rate (Solow). The release is a one-time event, stretched over a transition period of 5-10 years, after which the rate returns to the norm of a developed economy. It is not +3% every year forever.

Second: this is the removal of an INTERNAL brake. AB-EXIT eliminates management stupidity and corruption but does not control external factors (global crisis, commodity prices, demographics, war). Correct formulation: "removes the primary internal brake," not "guarantees growth."

Third: the state does not achieve 100% business efficiency. It has functions that business does not (defense, justice, care for the insolvent). It can approach the efficiency of a pizzeria, but cannot equal it. There is a release of value, but it is not complete.

With these caveats, the formulation changes from "AB-EXIT will provide +3-5%" (a populist promise) to "AB-EXIT can release +3-5% provided it brings management close to business efficiency" (a defensible assessment). The difference is in a single word — but it is the difference between science and marketing.

### 59.6. The Anti-Peronist Irony of Honest Assessment

There is an irony here that a skeptical economist would appreciate: AB-EXIT promises REALISTICALLY ("can, provided, potential, level effect"), whereas populism promises GUARANTEED results ("I will give everyone everything now"). The very honesty of the assessment with its caveats is an anti-populist marker. Peron promised the moon. AB-EXIT shows a calculation with three caveats. Those who have built their position on "I speak the bitter truth while others lie" will recognize their own style in this discipline.

### 59.7. The Pizzeria Analogy — Why the Gap is Real

The efficiency gap between state and business is not theoretical. A CEO of a pizzeria for embezzling $200M = 20 years in prison; a mayor of a city for the same $200M = often nothing. A Papa John's managed with the efficiency of a bankrupt city = dead within 5 years. A corporation under the pressure of the market, audits, shareholders, and competition is structurally more efficient than a monopolistic state without these pressures.

AB-EXIT does not turn the state into a pizzeria (the state has a different function). But it adds to the state the pressures it currently lacks: a dashboard = audit and transparency; a motivated electorate = shareholders demanding results; a dividend tied to the median = a metric of efficiency; a third party = competition. Each pressure narrows the gap. The sum of these pressures = the release of the very 20-30% currently being lost.

This is why +2% is the floor, not the ceiling. If all five pressures are working, the state moves toward business efficiency, and the value release approaches the upper limit of the calculation.

## 60. Chichvarkin: The Stung Voter, the Agitator, and the "Vote Retains Capital" Chain

### 60.1. Case Study and Diagnosis

Evgeny Chichvarkin (libertarian, London, wine business in Mayfair) in a 2026 interview describes the flight of the productive class from fiscal predation. Specifics: up to 16,500 millionaires left Britain in a single year (for Dubai, Zurich, Milan, Cyprus) following the abolition of non-dom status and the introduction of a tax of up to 45% on worldwide income. The result — the collapse of the luxury market, real estate, and high-end restaurants.

His mechanism for the problem — is Sections 56, 58, and 59 on the "top floor": a leftist government raises taxes → the productive leave → the tax base shrinks → less revenue is collected (he directly references the Laffer Curve: "with the tax hike, less tax revenue began to be collected") → politicians don't understand because they "deny mathematics and human nature" → they drive the country into a wall (Detroit, strikes) → only then "the next Thatcher" appears. A pendulum without an exit — the Argentine loop (Section 58) in a British performance.

His observation on electoral dynamics coincides verbatim with Sections 53/56: "people understand the rhetoric that the state owes them — so pensions grow, social spending grows; that's why leftists are popular worldwide." The majority votes for distribution; the minority that pays loses the vote.

### 60.2. Key Correction: "Against All" is the Cry of the Stung, Not the Yawn of the Apathetic

A superficial reading: Chichvarkin, when asked "who to vote for," answers "against all, they are all repulsive" — meaning he is an apathetic who has left the system. This is incorrect.

He lists everyone by name and explains why each is bad: the right-wing populists — no; the left — no; the Tories, who copied the leftist agenda — no; the Greens — "they need a psychiatrist"; Farage — "too much of a populist." This is not the apathy of "I don't care, someone will buy a Bentley" (as the non-voter said in Katz's video). This is the position of a person who WANTS to vote but has no one to vote for.

The distinction is fundamental. The apathetic does not participate because he is indifferent. The stung (Section 53) does not participate because there is no one worthy on the ballot — only populists from different sides, not a single intellectual. Hayek once taught in London; now, according to Chichvarkin, there are no libertarians in politics at all. He is motivated, disappointed, suffering from learned helplessness — but he is not indifferent.

### 60.3. Why the "Smart Ones" Are Not on the Ballot — and How AB-EXIT Returns Them

An intellectual candidate is currently electorally unviable. The electorate consists of the majority of distribution recipients (as Chichvarkin put it: "the rhetoric that the state owes them is understandable to the majority"). An intellectual saying "let's reduce distribution and heal the economy" loses to the distributor. Therefore, smart people do not enter politics — the game is a losing one. Only populists from both sides remain.

AB-EXIT inverts this mathematics. Distribution recipients take the dividend and leave; the motivated "calculators" receive a vote ×3. The intellectual's audience (calculators, productive, stung) now carries three times the weight; the populist's audience (distribution-oriented) has left for the money. The intellectual candidate is viable for the first time. Demand arises — and supply follows. Smart people enter politics because the game is finally winnable.

And then Chichvarkin finds someone to vote for. His "against all" disappears not out of resignation, but because there is finally someone worthy on the ballot. The stung voter returns — exactly the mechanism of Section 53.

### 60.4. From "Against All" to Free Turnout Agitator

Chichvarkin won't take the dividend — that money is laughable to him. He will choose the vote ×3. And he won't just vote — he is the type who AGITATES. He started a YouTube channel, walks through Mayfair explaining the Laffer Curve, argues, convinces, hires security, and sets business policy. He is a natural-born agitator.

Under AB-EXIT, he will walk around the neighborhood for free, calling on fellow "calculators" to go vote ×3 — because now it makes sense, now there is someone to vote for. AB-EXIT turns him from "against all, mentally moving to Dubai" into an active, free agitator for the turnout of the motivated. This is Section 56.8, Mechanism 5 (the third party becomes viable) in a living face: a viable intellectual candidate + a motivated agitator working for free out of self-interest.

### 60.5. The "Vote Retains Capital" Chain

An important clarification of the boundary. Chichvarkin is split into the voter and the capital. As a voter, he will return for the vote ×3 and will agitate. But his MONEY is mobile: with a tax of 45%, capital will flee to Dubai even if the vote returns to London.

The superficial conclusion: "AB-EXIT is powerless against mobile capital." This is incomplete. More accurately — AB-EXIT retains capital not directly, but indirectly, through the full chain:

AB-EXIT returns Chichvarkin-the-voter (vote ×3 + worthy candidates) → he votes for the intellectual and agitates for him for free → the intellectual-politician gains power and reduces fiscal predation → Chichvarkin-the-capital stays because the reason for fleeing has vanished.

The vote pulls the money behind it. A person is a citizen first, then a wallet. Without AB-EXIT, the first link breaks — and then both the vote leaves (into "against all") and the capital leaves (to Dubai). With AB-EXIT, the entire link is restored: the returned voter fixes the reason why the capital was fleeing.

This corrects the naive formulation "the mechanism doesn't work for the mobile elite." It does — but indirectly: it doesn't hold money by force, but returns to its owner the tool to fix what is driving the money away. Chichvarkin-the-voter saves Chichvarkin-the-capital.

### 60.6. Synthesis

Chichvarkin confirms the diagnosis of Sections 56, 58, and 59 at the top level of society (millionaires, not Michael with a €300 deficit): the tax state squeezes out the productive, the majority votes for distribution, a pendulum without an exit, enlightenment only through the wall.

And he is a living illustration of the cure. He is not an apathetic, but one who has been stung; AB-EXIT returns him to active citizenship, makes the intellectual candidate viable, and turns the disillusioned into an agitator. And the chain "vote → worthy politician → reduced predation → remaining capital" shows that AB-EXIT retains even the mobile elite — not through direct coercion, but through returned agency.

As in the case of Katz (the section on politicization through catastrophe): Chichvarkin believes that enlightenment comes only through the wall, through crisis. AB-EXIT contests this — it provides the "politics → money" connection and a worthy candidate without the need to smash into a concrete wall. One does not need to drive London to Detroit's level to return smart management to it.

## 61. The Field of Democracy Reform Ideas: Why Competitors Have No Door

### 61.1. Purpose of This Section

An honest position requires a review of alternatives. The claim that "AB-EXIT is the best idea" is unprovable and alienates a serious reader. The claim that "AB-EXIT is the only idea with a built-in path to implementation" is defensible and verifiable. This section pays tribute to competing ideas, analyzes them based on a single criterion (feasibility, not beauty), and positions AB-EXIT not as the cleverest design, but as a rare instance of reform that carries its own door.

### 61.2. Six Major Ideas and Their Common Flaw

**Deliberative Democracy (Habermas, Fishkin).** Citizens' assemblies, selected by lot, discuss a problem and provide recommendations. Tested: Ireland (abortion), France (climate). The flaw: recommendations are advisory. The French climate assembly gave proposals — Macron took some and discarded the rest. It has no teeth, no binding power.

**Sortition / Lottocracy (Van Reybrouck, "Against Elections").** Replacing elections with lots, like juries: random citizens rule in turns. The implementation flaw is fatal: who will vote to abolish their own right to vote in favor of a lottery? Current politicians — never. There is no path from point A (now) to point B (sortition). Van Reybrouck honestly provides no answer on HOW to introduce this.

**Quadratic Voting (Weyl, Posner, "Radical Markets").** You buy votes at a quadratic price, expressing the intensity of preference. The flaw: it requires society to accept "buying votes" (intuitively repulsive to most) and to rewrite the entire electoral infrastructure. Where is the referendum that would pass this? There is no path.

**Liquid Democracy.** You delegate your vote to someone you trust on a topic and can revoke it. The flaw: it works in experiments (Pirate Parties), but when scaled, it leads to the concentration of delegations among a few "super-voters" — i.e., the same oligarchy — plus there is no mechanism for adoption at the national level.

**Compulsory Voting (Australia).** A fine for non-attendance; turnout is around 90%. The flaw: the only one truly implemented (Australia has proven it for 100 years), but it DOES NOT solve the problem — it forces apathetics to the polls, where they vote randomly or for the loudest populist. It amplifies populism rather than curing it. It is feasible but counterproductive.

**Civic Education (Yudin's position, LSE).** Awakening the civic spirit, fostering engagement, lowering the voting age to 16. The flaw: there is no mechanism, and it works over generations. Post-democratic critique has proclaimed this goal since 2004 (Crouch) and 2008 (Wolin) — during this time, turnout has fallen, while inequality and populism have grown. A precise diagnosis without a tool.

### 61.3. The Pattern: Vision Without a Door

All these ideas have a VISION, but none have a DOOR. They describe a better room but do not show how to get there from the current room when the door is guarded by those for whom the transition is disadvantageous. This is a structural flaw of the entire field: democracy reform is proposed by those who do not control the mechanism of its adoption to those who do control it and are not interested.

The field is skewed toward diagnosis. Diagnosticians of the crisis are many (Crouch, Wolin, Yudin, Brzezinski, dozens of books). Constructive proposals with a path to implementation — almost none.

### 61.4. How AB-EXIT Differs

AB-EXIT carries its own door and its own lock:

The Door (Implementation Mechanism): A referendum that everyone will support because everyone sees a personal benefit — money or a vote weight of ×3. Unlike sortition (no one will give up their vote to a lottery) and QV (no one will accept "buying votes"), AB-EXIT asks no one to sacrifice — it gives to everyone. Therefore, it passes the vote that other ideas cannot.

The Lock (Irreversibility Mechanism, Section 58): After adoption, the filtered electorate does not vote to abolish its own benefit; the self-tightening loop makes legal reversal impossible.

This doesn't make AB-EXIT smarter or fairer than deliberation or sortition. It makes it the only one that can be brought to a real trial.

### 61.5. The Honest Boundary of the Assertion

Three disciplining caveats, without which this would be propaganda:

First: "no better found" is not equal to "no better exists." Perhaps a better idea exists with someone unanalyzed or will emerge tomorrow. The absence of a found counter-example is not proof of its non-existence.

Second: AB-EXIT wins on feasibility, but has NOT been tested for effectiveness. Deliberation has been tested in Ireland, compulsory voting in Australia; their effects are known. AB-EXIT has not been launched anywhere. Its superiority is on paper, like the others. The only difference is that it has a plausible path to a pilot, so it can be TESTED, not that it is proven to work.

Third: The risk of an echo chamber. This document builds AB-EXIT over dozens of sections; naturally, it wins in its own analysis. A defender of sortition could take down AB-EXIT with arguments not considered here just as easily as sortition was analyzed here. Therefore, the assertion is not tested by a friendly court, but by a hostile one — and a hostile critic (like Yudin) is invaluable.

### 61.6. Precise Formulation of the Position

Not "AB-EXIT is the best idea for improving democracy."

But rather: "AB-EXIT is the only known idea for democracy reform that carries within it its own mechanism for implementation and irreversibility. Others describe a desired state but provide no feasible path to it when the transition is disadvantageous to the current powers. Therefore, AB-EXIT is not proven to be the best, but the only one feasible enough that it can even be tested in practice."

The project's niche is not "I am smarter than everyone," but "I am in a rare part of the field that doesn't just describe the illness but offers an operable treatment with a real path to the operating room." Occupying this niche honestly is stronger than claiming absolute superiority.

## 62. AB-EXIT as a Consensus Topic, Not a Wedge: Why the First Referendum Triggers a Politicians' Race

### 62.1. The Analogy Error: Wedge vs. Consensus

It is tempting to evaluate AB-EXIT's implementation path by analogy with same-sex marriage or marijuana legalization: local ballot initiatives against federal law, a slow accumulation of pressure, years before change. This analogy is false, and the error in it is systemic.

Same-sex marriage and marijuana are WEDGE issues. Support them — and half the electorate is yours, while half is against you. Politicians were cautious for years, waiting for a shift in polls before taking the risk. Eleven years from Massachusetts (2004) to Obergefell (2015) — not because the mechanism was slow, but because the topic divided people and politicians feared losing half.

## 63. The Hypocrisy of Sacredness and Selective Protection: Why You Can Sell Your Body and Lose at a Casino, but Cannot Take Money for Non-Participation

### 63.1. The Doctrine Examined in This Section

American law blocks AB-EXIT through the Unconstitutional Conditions Doctrine (Koontz v. St. Johns): the government cannot condition the granting of a benefit (money) on the waiver of a constitutional right (the vote). The justification is paternalistic: the "voluntariness" of a poor person who needs money is declared illusory — the poor allegedly agree under the pressure of necessity, thus the exchange is unfree.

This section shows that the doctrine is applied SELECTIVELY and HYPOCRITICALLY — through two analogies and a breakdown of the only evasion left to the court.

### 63.2. The First Blow: The Sacredness of the Vote that Devalued the Vote

The vote has been declared sacred and inalienable — so much so that one cannot receive compensation for it. The result of this "sacredness" in practice:
— half of the citizens do not go to vote;
— "politics" has become a synonym for "the dirty business of liars";
— everyone knows this and can change nothing.

Sacredness did not protect the vote — it devalued it. By declaring the vote so holy that it cannot be disposed of, the system has brought it to the point where it is simply not used — for free, silently, en masse. The "sacred" vote has turned into a piece of paper that half of the people throw away without looking.

What is declared priceless, in practice, costs zero. The poor currently either give up the vote for free (by not going) or give it to a liar (by voting for a manipulator). Both outcomes are worse than honestly taking compensation or voting with an undiluted weight. AB-EXIT doesn't make the vote cynical — it shows that the vote IS ALREADY devalued and offers to return its value.

An additional asymmetry: the vote is "inalienable" when a CITIZEN wants to dispose of it, but perfectly alienable when the STATE wants to take it away. The USA disenfranchises about 4 million people through felony disenfranchisement — without any compensation. Sacredness is turned on selectively: against the citizen, not against the state.

### 63.3. The Second Blow: Selective Protection (Prostitution, Casinos, Lotteries)

The doctrine "the poor cannot voluntarily decide on sacred matters under the pressure of necessity" is tested against other transactions where the poor decide under the same pressure:

**Prostitution.** At stake is the body, sexual integrity — something most traditions consider more sacred than the vote. A poor woman "agrees" under the pressure of necessity — exactly the "illusion of voluntariness" according to the court's logic. And yet, dozens of jurisdictions (Netherlands, Germany, Nevada, New Zealand) have legalized this, recognizing the right of an adult to dispose of even their body.

**Casinos and Lotteries.** A mathematically guaranteed loss: the chance of winning is a few percent, the house always wins. The poor go there under the pressure of the same necessity with a near-zero chance. The state doesn't just allow it — it RUNS the lotteries itself and taxes the casinos, i.e., it PROFITS from the loss of the poor.

The comparison:
— Prostitution: the poor give up their body (more sacred than the vote) — legal.
— Casino/Lottery: the poor give up money with a guaranteed loss — legal, the state profits.
— AB-EXIT: the poor RECEIVE money for what they were already doing for free (not voting) — a crime.

In prostitution and casinos, the poor LOSE (body, money). In AB-EXIT, the poor GAIN. The law permits transactions where the poor are at a loss and forbids the transaction where the poor are at a gain — under the banner of "protecting the poor."

Conclusion: the "illusion of voluntariness" doctrine is applied selectively. When the poor lose (casinos) — their voluntariness is recognized. When the poor gain (AB-EXIT) — it is declared illusory. Protection is activated exactly when the poor would be better off. This is not protection — it is the codification of the second-class status of the poor as a deciding subject: the rich are allowed to waive rights for money everywhere (NDAs, arbitration clauses, selling image rights), the poor in the matter of the vote — are not.

### 63.4. The Court's Only Evasion and Three Responses to It

The court will not be able to beat the analogy on the line of "voluntariness" or "sacredness." Only one distinction remains: prostitution and casinos affect only the person himself (his body, his money), while the vote is participation in the management of the COMMON, and a waiver affects others. The logic: "if you lose, you suffer; if you don't vote for money, you change the composition of the electorate, and that harms the collective."

**Response 1.** Prostitution and casinos ALSO affect others. A gambler ruins a family, children go hungry — direct harm to third parties. Prostitution affects public health and families. The state tolerates this. Thus "effect on others" is not an absolute prohibition — the state allows vices that harm others all the time.

**Response 2.** AB-EXIT affects others LESS than the status quo. Currently, the poor either do not vote (the same effect on the electorate, but for free) or vote under the manipulation of a liar (harming the common decision by choosing the worst). AB-EXIT does not create new harm to the collective — the same person still doesn't participate, only consciously and with compensation, while the remaining ones vote undiluted. The effect on the "common" is either the same or better.

**Response 3.** Casinos and lotteries take money away from the poor that they could have spent on participation in society and give it to the state/corporation — an extraction of their "share in the common" in favor of others. The state profits from this. Where is the protection of the collective?

### 63.5. Distinguishing Tammany Hall and AB-EXIT (Why the Law is Overbroad)

The law "you cannot pay for non-participation" (the boilerplate "vote or refrain from voting" in all 50 states) was written against a specific evil:

Tammany Hall / Jim Crow: A PRIVATE entity (party machine, boss) SECRETLY pays the opponent's voters to stay home to STEAL the result from the majority. The victim is the people. Fraud against the will of the people.

AB-EXIT: THE STATE pays OPENLY, according to the decision of the MAJORITY ITSELF in a referendum. There is no private manipulator, no secret, no stolen result, no victim. The fulfillment of the will of the people.

These are opposite phenomena under the same wording. The law suffers from overbreadth: written against a private secret theft of will, it accidentally catches an open decision of the people about themselves. The argument in court is not to "break the law," but to "narrow the overbroad norm so that it doesn't capture what it wasn't aimed at."

The counter-argument (the majority could vote for the suppression of the minority by cutting off the poor from the ballot for money) is removed by AB-EXIT's mechanics: the poor person chooses for themselves, can vote with an undiluted weight, no one decides for them; the option is symmetrical for everyone and not aimed at a specific group.

### 63.6. Purpose of the Section

This is not a proof that AB-EXIT is legal today. An unfair law remains in effect until it is narrowed by a court or changed by pressure. This is an arsenal for three audiences:

**Bipartisanship / "Across the Aisle"** — "making the left and right agree." A process, not a result. A voter cannot touch "agreement." It sounds like "we will be less angry" — a virtue, not a proposal.

**"Common Sense," "The Sensible Majority"** — Manchin's slogan verbatim ("fight for America's sensible majority"). This is not an idea, but a posture.

The outcome of the inventory: procedure (RCV) + pain (budget) + posture (common sense). Not a single proposal that an ordinary person would hear and think "I will personally get something right now."

### 67.4. The Structural Curse of Centrism

This is not the laziness of specific politicians. It is a built-in contradiction.

Centrism by definition is "moderation," "balance," "no extremes." Moderation does not give birth to bright ideas because a bright idea is always radical in form (even if sensible in substance). A centrist fears a radical form — it contradicts his brand as "calm and sensible." Therefore, he is doomed to bland ideas.

The left can offer "take from billionaires" — bright. The right — "build a wall" — bright. A centrist structurally cannot offer anything brighter than "let's all agree." One cannot simultaneously be "moderate in positioning" and "bright in idea." This is the curse.
