30 days before every election, every citizen receives a simple choice through an app, website, or mail. Two options:

**Step 3: What happens to those who took the money.**

**Step 4: Automatic restoration.**

---

**KEY PROTECTION MECHANISMS**

**Floating Rate.** The compensation amount is not fixed arbitrarily by someone. It is calculated automatically as a small percentage of the country's median income. The formula is fixed by the referendum. This means:

In a wealthy country (median income $5,000/month), the amount will be, say, $500—insignificant for most. Few will take the money; the elections will proceed almost as usual. The system is "on pause."

In a poor country (median income $500/month), the amount will be, say, $50—noticeable for the poor, but not critical. Some people will take the money, the electorate will shrink, and the quality of voting will rise.

As the economy grows, the amount grows, but people get wealthier faster—the incentive to take the money weakens. If the economy falls, the amount falls along with incomes. The system self-calibrates.

**Vote Weighting.** This is the key effect that makes YUNO unique. When part of the citizens take compensation and exit the voting, the electorate shrinks. And the vote of each person remaining mathematically carries more weight.

Example: there are 3 million voters in a country. One person's vote weighs 1/3,000,000. If 50% took the compensation, 1.5 million remain. This is not a metaphor—it's mathematics.

Denis, here's what I found. And this is very good news for YUNO.

Grok was wrong—or, more accurately, greatly oversimplified. The "one person, one vote" principle does not block YUNO. Here is why.

**What "one person, one vote" actually means:**

This principle, established by the Supreme Court in Reynolds v. Sims (1964), concerns a specific thing: equality of population in electoral districts when redistricting. It requires districts to contain roughly the same number of people so that the vote of a resident in one district does not weigh more than the vote of a resident in another.
