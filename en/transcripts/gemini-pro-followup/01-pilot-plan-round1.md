## 0. What is AB-EXIT (Context in 5 lines)

Before elections, every registered voter chooses: to vote (with increased vote weight, as part of the electorate has opted out) OR receive a Civic Dividend (~$780) from budget savings and not vote in this cycle. These are mutually exclusive options. Voluntary. Reversible each cycle (Cycle Lock). Declared as legally distinct from vote-buying: no candidate benefits from a voter's exit.

---

## 1. Pilot Goal

NOT to implement AB-EXIT nationwide. The goal is more modest and specific: to hold ONE municipal referendum in ONE small town and obtain figures (percentage FOR + turnout) that are impossible to ignore. Hypothesis: strong figures trigger a chain reaction (underdogs → press → federal pioneer politician → pressure on the law). Weak figures — the pilot fails, the chain reaction does not start.

Metric of success: the referendum is placed on the ballot AND gathers >65% FOR with a turnout higher than the average for this type of election. Metric of failure: it does not reach the ballot, OR <55% FOR, OR turnout is lower than usual.

---

## 2. THE MAIN LEGAL BARRIER (Honestly, at the beginning)

**52 U.S.C. § 10307(c):** a felony — to pay or offer payment for registration or voting. Fine up to $10,000 or 5 years in prison. Interpreted broadly: "any incentive is considered a payment, even a cookie." Applies to elections WHERE THERE IS A FEDERAL CANDIDATE ON THE BALLOT (President, Senate, House of Representatives).

Precedent-warning: Musk's offer ($1M to petition signers, 2024) was called by a leading election law attorney "exactly what the statute was created to criminalize."

**Circumvention (not violation, but moving out of scope):**
- Pilot ONLY in purely municipal elections in an off-year, where there is NO federal position on the ballot. There, 10307(c) does not apply.
- Plus, check STATE law on bribery/incentives (often broader than federal) — choose a state with soft wording.
- Plus, legal reclassification: "Civic Dividend from budget savings for all voters," where non-voting is an option, not a condition for payment. Weaker than the first two; the court looks at the substance.

**HONESTLY:** whether the reclassification will hold up in court is UNKNOWN. This is the weakest link in the plan. An election-law attorney is needed BEFORE the first signature.

## 3. City Selection Criteria (Three filters simultaneously)

The city must pass ALL three:
1. **Home-rule charter city with the right of citizen initiative.** 48 states grant a form of local initiative; home-rule cities have the strongest right (initiative, referendum, charter amendment). AB-EXIT changes election mechanics → this is a charter amendment, not a simple ordinance.
2. **Off-year municipal elections without a federal position on the ballot** (evading 10307(c)).
3. **Soft state law on incentives for voting.**

Additional preferences (for strong figures):
- Small town (low absolute signature threshold; NOT Los Angeles — there 15% = ~300,000 signatures, unattainable).
- Budget crisis in the city (then "dividend from savings" = solution, not a handout).
- Strong tradition of direct democracy (Colorado, Oregon, Washington — candidates for verification).

**HONESTLY:** a specific city has not been chosen. A legal map is needed: the intersection of three filters may yield a very narrow list or an empty one. This must be checked before anything else.

## 4. Underdog Profile (Not a name)

I am NOT naming a specific living politician (risk for them and for the project). Profile:
- Mayor or council member of a small town, losing to the establishment, who has nothing to lose and needs a radical differentiator.
- Reformist/libertarian leaning.
- City with a budget crisis.

Logic: AB-EXIT for an underdog is a ready-made differentiator. Even if the referendum is overturned, the underdog gains popularity as a "defender of the people against the feds." Both success and cancellation work for them.

**HONESTLY:** the underdog is sought AFTER choosing the city, for a specific local situation. Right now, this is a vacancy, not a plan.

## 5. Five Launch Phases

**Phase 1. Legal Packaging.** Hire a local election-law attorney. Frame AB-EXIT as a charter amendment "Civic Dividend & Enhanced Vote," bypassing the "payment for non-voting" phrasing. Check state law. WITHOUT this — do not proceed.

**Phase 2. The Needle's Eye: Signature Collection.** Collect signatures to place it on the ballot (the main battle — see section 58 of the main document). Viral campaign "Your vote is worth money." Collecting signatures for a referendum is legal (First Amendment), it is NOT bribery.

**Phase 3. Underdog as the Face.** A local outsider politician takes this as a platform of distinction.

**Phase 4. Voting.** Winning is easy IF it reaches the ballot (everyone sees the benefit). The goal is strong figures (>65% FOR, increased turnout).

**Phase 5. Defense/Leveraging the Outcome.** Two scenarios: (a) it stands → AB-EXIT is implemented; (b) overturned by a court → scandal "feds took away the people's money" → underdog-hero → pressure on 10307(c). Both advance the cause.

## 6. THE CRITICAL BOUNDARY (Where the legal ends and prison begins)

- **Legal and strong:** referendum FOR an idea, campaign, signature collection, provoking judicial reversal, scandal. People vote for policy — no one goes to jail.
- **Prison:** actual PAYMENT of money for non-voting BEFORE the mechanism is legalized, especially in elections with a federal position. This is a crime under 10307(c). A specific organizer goes to jail.

Rule: fight with ideas and voting — the machine works. Hand out cash prematurely — you go to jail and bury the topic.

## 7. EXPLICIT LIST OF WEAK POINTS (For evaluator attack)

1. Legal reclassification "dividend ≠ payment for non-voting" may not hold up in court. The weakest link.
2. Intersection of three city selection filters may yield an empty list.
3. Off-year municipal elections have LOW turnout by definition — how then to show "increased turnout" as a success? Possible internal metric conflict.
4. Source of Civic Dividend funding in the pilot: "budget savings" in a small town with a budget crisis — where does the money physically come from in the first cycle, BEFORE the savings have accumulated? A chicken-and-egg problem.
5. Underdog-vacancy: there is no plan until there is a person.
6. Assumption that "strong figures will trigger a federal chain" — not tested, based on analogy with consensus topics.
7. State laws of 50 states have not been checked individually.
8. Cycle Lock and accounting for vote weights require technical counting infrastructure that a small town lacks.
