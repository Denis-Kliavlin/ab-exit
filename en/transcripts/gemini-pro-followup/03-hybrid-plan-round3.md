## 2. Legal Packaging — Two Forms (Your Evaluation Needed)

**Form A — Citizen-initiated statute, amending the state's vote-bribery law.**
Text: Narrow the phrasing of "vote or refrain from voting" in the state's penal code, excluding open compensated abstention by the will of the people through a state mechanism.
Effect if passed: AB-EXIT becomes legal in the state.
Risk: More heavily attacked as "legalizing bribery."

**Form B — Citizen-initiated statute, obligating the legislature to consider.**
Text: Obligate the legislature within N months to hold public hearings / create a commission / place an advisory question on the next election.
Effect if passed: Legal status does not change directly, but a mandatory process is created.
Risk: Softer, but the effect is weaker (the legislature can "consider and reject").

## 3. Funding — A New Model (Important Correction)

The previous draft considered $5-10M as a killing constraint. Correction: The model is not "mass crowdfunding," but a HYBRID.

Anchor funding: One or two major donors cover 70-80% of the budget (professional signature gathering, lawyers, operations). There exists a layer of people in the world for whom $5-10M is an acceptable stake for the sake of historical primacy in democratic reform (crypto-billionaires with libertarian convictions, retired tech founders with a penchant for political experimentation, emigrated entrepreneurs with capital and ideological anger—the Chichvarkin-type, but larger). AB-EXIT is party-neutral (Section 62 is a consensus theme), which makes it attractive for donors who do not want to be labeled left/right.

Parallel mass fundraising: Thousands of small donations cover 20-30% AND provide the NARRATIVE that "this is a grassroots initiative," without which a large donor becomes a point of attack ("the initiative of Billionaire X"). Money is needed from one; legitimacy is needed from thousands.

New main constraint of this stage: Not "finding $5-10M," but "finding and convincing ONE anchor donor with a historical motive" + simultaneously building a mass base for the narrative.

# ZONE A — LEGAL ISSUES (I ask for a strict state-by-state verification)

A1. **Pre-election review for Form A.** In how many of the 26 states with citizen initiative can a vote-bribery statute be amended through a citizen initiative WITHOUT pre-election content review? A2. **Precedents for changing election law through citizen initiative.** Have there been successful citizen-initiated changes specifically to vote-bribery / campaign finance / election crime statutes in the US in the last 20 years? Besides RCV (which is not an election crime). I ask for specific cases with the statutes.

A3. **Form B — admissibility.** Can a citizen initiative in Oregon / California / Colorado obligate the legislature to hold hearings or issue an advisory question? Or do statutes obligating the legislature to act violate the state's separation of powers?

A4. **Single-state effect.** If Oregon passes Form A, does this apply in Oregon to elections with a federal candidate on the ballot? Or will 10307(c) preempt the state law? If it preempts—does AB-EXIT in its passed form only work for state-local off-year elections in Oregon? Confirm or refute.

A5. **Precedents of pre-emption.** Have there been cases in the US in the last 30 years where a state initiative contradicted federal election law and was overturned by a federal court AFTER adoption? What happened to the initiators?

A6. **Oregon signature threshold.** Is ~117,000 the exact figure for a statutory initiative in 2026? Deadlines for gathering, requirements for distribution by district, the percentage of rejected signatures?
